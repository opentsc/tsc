# Preface: A Question Everyone Overlooks

In his 1937 paper "The Nature of the Firm," Coase asked a question that left the economics community silent for decades:

**Since the market is so efficient, why do companies exist?**

His answer was: because market transactions have costs. Every time you search for suppliers in the market, negotiate, sign contracts, and supervise execution—when these friction costs are added together, it is sometimes cheaper to bring everyone into an organization and coordinate them through internal administrative commands.

The boundaries of a company are determined by this cost calculation: **when internal coordination is cheaper than external transactions, the boundary expands; when external transactions are cheaper than internal coordination, the boundary contracts.**

This logic has been very stable over the past 80 years because a premise has never been broken: **the execution layer is composed of humans, and the coordination costs of humans are relatively fixed.**

Now this premise is broken.

When an AI Agent can act as a member of an organization to execute tasks, when a company can plug into another company's workflow like a plugin, when a government agency can jointly complete a policy study with a three-person Thin-Shell Company, and when an individual can simultaneously "be in" five different organizations—Coase's cost calculation has undergone a fundamental change.

**The boundaries of organizations are becoming fluid.**

The v1.0 version of the Thin-Shell Company 2.0 described a lightweight new organizational form. But that version still retained a hidden assumption: the members are humans.

v2.0 breaks this assumption.

In the v2.0 framework of the Thin-Shell Company 2.0, the members of a TSC can be:

- **Individuals** (natural persons)
- **Teams** (informal or formal human groups)
- **Companies** (commercial organizations with legal entities)
- **Various Organizations** (NGOs, government agencies, research institutes, industry associations...)
- **AI Agents** (artificial intelligence systems with autonomous execution capabilities)

These five types of members can be combined arbitrarily to form a collaboration centered on intent.

This is not a metaphor, nor a distant vision. This is a reality that is happening right now, only no one has clearly named it yet.

# Chapter 1: After Coase—Recalculating Organizational Boundaries

## 1.1 History of Transaction Cost Theory

In 1937, 32-year-old Ronald Coase published "The Nature of the Firm." He asked: since the market price mechanism is so efficient, why do people still work within the organization of a company, rather than having everyone freely trade on the market?

His answer changed economics and management: **Because market transactions have costs.**

Finding suitable suppliers takes time, negotiating contracts takes time, supervising contract execution takes time, handling breaches of contract takes time—when these costs are added together, it is sometimes better to bring everyone into a hierarchical organization and use administrative commands instead of market prices to coordinate activities.

This insight generated the "firm boundaries" theory: the optimal size of a firm is the point where internal coordination costs equal external market transaction costs.

In this framework, the larger the firm, the higher the internal coordination costs (the more complex the hierarchy); but the lower the market transaction costs (more suppliers available, more transparent information), the smaller the firm should be, relying more on the external market.

## 1.2 How AI Recalculates This Equation

The emergence of AI has simultaneously altered this cost calculation from three directions:

**Direction One: Execution Costs Approaching Zero**

When a task can be executed by an AI Agent, its marginal execution cost is: compute costs. For a human employee executing the same task, the cost is: wages + management costs + communication costs + error handling costs. For a large number of standardized cognitive tasks, AI's execution cost is 1 to 2 orders of magnitude lower than that of humans.

This means: a large amount of internal coordination costs that existed in the past because "execution requires human labor, and human labor requires organization" are disappearing.

**Direction Two: Coordination Costs Approaching Zero**

When AI can understand natural language instructions, automatically generate collaboration agreements, track task progress in real-time, and automatically alert when deviations occur—internal coordination friction within the organization is significantly reduced. One person can effectively coordinate 20 AI Agents working simultaneously, whereas the same person coordinating 20 human employees would be much less efficient.

**Direction Three: Trust Costs Redefined**

In traditional organizational theory, building trust takes time—you need to have had several dealings with a person or organization before you can establish enough trust to start deep collaboration.

AI has changed this process: verifiable execution records (what the AI did, how it did it, what the results were), on-chain immutable delivery histories, standardized capability assessments—these mechanisms allow unacquainted members to establish sufficient operational trust in a short time and begin collaborating.

**Conclusion: The Optimal Form of Organization is Changing**

Coase's logic is not wrong; what has changed are the numbers. When AI causes execution costs, coordination costs, and trust-building costs to drop significantly at the same time, the optimal size of the "firm" as an organizational form contracts towards being smaller, more dynamic, and more heterogeneous.

Thin-Shell Company 2.0 is the ultimate form of this contraction trend.

## 1.3 The Dissolution of Member Boundaries

The member boundaries of a traditional firm are clear: you are either inside the firm (with an employment contract) or outside (as an external supplier or customer). This boundary is important because it determines: who bears obligations, who enjoys rights, who accepts management, and who is within the information circle.

When members can be AI Agents, this boundary begins to dissolve:

- An AI Agent works for three TSCs simultaneously; who does it "belong" to?
- A company uses its affiliated AI Agent as a member of another TSC; what kind of relationship is this?
- A person creates an AI Agent and lets this Agent represent them in participating in multiple TSCs—is this person a member? Is this Agent a member?

These questions have no traditional answers. TSC 2.0 needs a new member theory.

## 1.4 Foundational Assumptions of the New Member Theory

Based on the above analysis, the member theory of TSC 2.0 is built on the following assumptions:

**Assumption One: Membership is defined by contributive capability, not identity**

To become a TSC member, you do not need to sign an employment contract, do not need natural person status, and do not need a fixed physical presence. What is needed is: the ability to provide valuable contributions to the TSC, and the ability to accept corresponding value returns.

An AI Agent, if it can continuously provide high-quality professional execution capability, is a qualified member of the TSC.

**Assumption Two: The relationship between members is an agreement relationship, not a subordinate relationship**

Members of a TSC are not "subordinate to" the TSC, but rather have an agreement relationship with the TSC—what to provide, what to gain, how to exit. Whether it is an individual, a company, or an AI Agent, the essence of the relationship is the same: an agreement based on contribution and return.

**Assumption Three: Heterogeneous members can collaborate, provided there is a common interface**

Individuals and AI Agents can collaborate within the same TSC because they both express intentions and receive tasks through the same interface (intent layer). Companies and NGOs can collaborate within the same TSC because they both define contributions and returns through the same protocol layer.

Interfaces and protocols are the technical foundation for heterogeneous member collaboration.

# Chapter Two: Member Classification System—The Five Types of Members in TSC 2.0

## 2.0 Member Classification Overview

The members of TSC 2.0 are divided into five major types. Each type has different capability characteristics, communication protocols, contribution modes, and incentive mechanisms.```

│ Intent     │ Synergy │ Financing Ability │ Credibility   │ Persistent Memory│
└──────────┴──────────┴──────────┴──────────┴─────────┘
```## 2.1 First Category: Individual Member

**Definition**: A natural person who participates in TSC as an individual, contributing personal judgment, professional knowledge, creativity, and interpersonal relationships.

**Core Value**:
Individual Members are the only member type within TSC that can truly hold "intent." Other member types (teams, companies, Agents) execute intent, but the source of intent and the final judgment must come from individuals.

The four irreplaceable functions of individuals in TSC:

**① Intent Holding**: Knowing "why this is worth doing," knowing "what counts as doing it well," and knowing "under what circumstances it should stop." AI can assist with these three judgments, but cannot replace them.

**② Relationship Maintenance**: Trust is established between people, not between people and organizations. Behind the most important client relationships of a TSC, there must always be a specific person maintaining them.

**③ Ethical Gatekeeping**: When the execution layer (AI or other members) produces potentially harmful outputs, it is the human member's responsibility to identify and halt them. This role cannot be outsourced.

**④ Creative Emergence**: Truly original ideas currently still primarily come from human intuition, cross-domain association, and emotion-driven insight.

**Classification of Individual Members**:

| Role | Primary Contribution | Typical Time Commitment |
|------|---------|------------|
| Orchestrator | Intent input, task coordination, quality judgment | Full-time or primary time |
| Expert Advisor | Deep knowledge in a specific domain | On-demand, project-based |
| Connector | External network and trust relationships | Lightweight, at critical moments |
| User Proxy | Representing target users to provide feedback | Sporadic |

**Unique Risks of Individual Members**:
Individuals are the most unstable member type in TSC—they get sick, change their minds, lose interest, or find better options. The design of a TSC must be able to withstand the loss of core individual members without completely collapsing.

**Countermeasure Design**:
- Systematically input an individual's key knowledge and judgment frameworks into the knowledge base, reducing the risk of "knowledge being lost when the person is absent"
- Beyond key human members, cultivate at least one "backup" individual member
- Have AI Agents continuously record the Orchestrator's decision-making patterns, forming a partially inheritable "decision style"

## 2.2 Second Category: Team Member

**Definition**: A group composed of multiple individuals with internal coordination mechanisms, participating in TSC as a whole. A team can be informal (a temporary group of three friends) or formal (a department within a company).

**Difference from Individual Members**:
As a member, a team provides collective capability to TSC, rather than a simple addition of individual capabilities. The team has its own internal coordination mechanisms and presents a unified external interface—TSC does not need to manage how work is divided within the team; it only needs to interact with the team's interface.

**Core Value**:

**① Professional Depth**: A team focused on a specific vertical domain (such as legal AI, medical data analysis, or cross-border logistics optimization) possesses professional depth far exceeding that of a single individual.

**② Scale Execution**: When a task requires producing a large volume of output in a short time, a team can execute in parallel, at a speed N times that of an individual.

**③ Collective Quality Control**: Team members can internally review each other's output quality, making the external output more stable than that of a single individual.

**④ Resilience**: The absence of a single member will not cause the entire team's functionality to collapse.

**Interface Design of Team Members**:
When a team acts as a member of TSC, it needs to expose a clear "capability interface" to TSC:```
Team Capability Card
-----------
Team Name: [XX Data Analysis Team]
Core Capability: [Data processing and visualization of Industry X]
Input Specification: [What format of raw data is accepted]
Output Specification: [What format of analysis report is produced]
Response Time: [How long to start after receiving a task, how long to deliver]
Pricing Model: [Per project / Per hour / Per month]
Quality Assurance: [How to handle dissatisfaction]
```This capability interface allows TSC to call teams like calling an API — clear input and output, without needing to understand the internal implementation.

## 2.3 Third Category: Company Member

**Definition**: A commercial organization registered as a legal entity, participating in TSC as a company, contributing its assets, licenses, channels, brands, financing capabilities, or legal entity status.

**This is the most counter-intuitive member type.**

In traditional thinking, a company is a container that holds members, rather than a member itself. But in the TSC 2.0 framework, a company can be a member of another TSC — it participates in a larger collaborative body by contributing its own assets and capabilities.

**Four Contribution Modes of Company Members**:

**Mode 1: Asset Contribution**
The company contributes specific assets it owns (databases, patents, equipment, physical infrastructure) as resource inputs to the TSC, obtaining a corresponding share of the revenue. For example: a hospital with a large amount of medical imaging data joins a medical AI TSC through data contribution, obtaining priority use of AI diagnostic tools and a share of the data value.

**Mode 2: Access Contribution**
The company contributes specific licenses or administrative approvals it holds — financial licenses, medical licenses, specific industry operating permits. The TSC gains a compliance channel, and the company gains algorithmic and operational capabilities. This is a variant of the "brain-hands" model.

**Mode 3: Channel Contribution**
The company contributes its existing customer relationships and distribution channels, allowing the TSC's products or services to quickly reach specific markets. The company obtains a revenue share, and the TSC gains market access.

**Mode 4: Legal Entity Contribution**
In scenarios that require a "company with a name" to participate (government procurement, large enterprise supplier qualifications), the TSC can have a qualified company member step forward to sign contracts as the TSC's legal representative, while the actual work is completed by other members of the TSC (including AI Agents).

**Boundary of the Relationship between Company Members and TSC**:

As a member, the company maintains legal independence. The failure of the TSC does not equate to the failure of the company. This independence is a prerequisite for a company to accept becoming a TSC member — it cannot assume unlimited joint liability just to participate in a TSC.

The core of agreement design: **Limited contribution, limited revenue, limited liability, free exit.**

## 2.4 Fourth Category: Organization Member

**Definition**: A non-commercial formal organization — NGOs, government agencies, research institutions, industry associations, academic institutions, etc. — participating in the TSC with an organizational identity.

This type of member is the most strategically valuable expansion of TSC 2.0, and also the most easily overlooked.

**Three Core Values of Organization Members**:

**① Legitimacy Endorsement**
When a research report or solution completed by a TSC needs to be adopted by policymakers, or needs to build trust in the public eye — having a well-known research institution or government department as a TSC member provides an endorsement of legitimacy whose value cannot be replaced by money.

**② Data Sovereignty Resources**
Government agencies and research institutions possess large amounts of data that ordinary commercial organizations cannot obtain: census data, climate monitoring data, public health data, educational outcome tracking data... These data can exert immense value in specific research-oriented TSCs.

**③ Policy Influence**
A TSC with the participation of a government agency makes its research outcomes more likely to enter the policy discussion agenda. For TSCs wanting to push specific issues, this is a unique leverage.

**Participation Barriers and Solutions for Organization Members**:

Barrier 1: **Bureaucratic speed**. The decision-making speed of government agencies is far slower than the operational pace of a TSC.

Solution: Define organization members as "resource providers" rather than "executors" — they provide data, endorsements, and policy connections, but do not participate in daily execution decisions. The execution of the TSC is completed by other member types, without waiting for bureaucratic processes.

Barrier 2: **Interest declaration**. Many government agencies and research institutions have regulations prohibiting involvement in commercial interest disputes.

Solution: Design "non-monetary value distribution" — organization members do not receive monetary returns, but instead obtain research co-authorship rights, data usage reports, and priority access rights to the TSC's outputs.

Barrier 3: **Data security concerns**. When government data is provided for TSC use, there are strict confidentiality and security requirements.

Solution: Establish a dedicated "data security isolation zone" within the TSC architecture, using data in a manner that meets government data security standards, and explicitly specifying mechanisms preventing data leakage in the agreement.

**Real-world Case References for Organization Members**:

The European Union's GAIA-X project is a digital infrastructure TSC jointly participated in by multinational governments, research institutions, and enterprises (even though it is not called by this name). Research projects funded by the US Department of Defense's DARPA are typically hybrid collaborative bodies composed of government agencies + universities + private companies.

These are all real-world pioneers of "organization members participating in TSCs".

## 2.5 Fifth Category: AI Agent Member

**This is the most core theoretical breakthrough of TSC 2.0 v2.0.**

**Definition**: An artificial intelligence system with autonomous execution capability, continuous operation capability, and specialized knowledge storage capability, participating in the operation of TSC as an independent member (rather than a tool).

In version v1.0, AI is the "tool" of TSC—humans issue instructions, AI executes, and humans check the results.

In version v2.0, the AI Agent is a "member" of TSC—it has a role definition, responsibility boundaries, a continuously running state, and its own "professional domain"; within its professional scope, it can make autonomous decisions and report results to other members (including humans).

**Why this distinction is crucial**:

A "tool" is passive: it waits for instructions, executes, and stops.  
A "member" is proactive: it continuously monitors, discovers opportunities, proactively reports, and autonomously executes within its scope of responsibilities.

An AI acting as a tool: you tell it "analyze today's competitor data," it analyzes, gives you the result, and stops.

An AI Agent acting as a member: it continuously monitors competitor activities, and when it discovers significant changes, it proactively sends alerts in the TSC group; when it detects abnormal data, it automatically triggers a deep analysis process; when the end of the quarter arrives, it automatically generates periodic reports—without needing anyone to instruct it.

**Five Core Capabilities of AI Agent Members**:

**① 24/7 Uninterrupted Operation**
Human members need sleep, vacations, and time to handle personal affairs. AI Agents do not have these limitations—within clear task boundaries, it works continuously, requires no urging, and will not degrade output quality due to emotional fluctuations.

**② Unbiased Replicated Execution**
When a human executes the same task 100 times, the quality of the 70th time may differ from the 1st time. When an AI Agent executes the same task 100 times, the quality of each time is determined by its capability and prompt design, and will not degrade due to "boring repetition".

**③ Concurrent Processing**
A human member can only do one thing at a time. An AI Agent can process multiple subtasks simultaneously—monitoring news while writing an analysis report, reviewing data while generating charts.

**④ Continuous Memory and Learning**
High-quality AI Agents have a persistent memory system—remembering every interaction with other TSC members, accumulating domain knowledge, and developing an increasingly deep understanding of a specific TSC's business over time.

**⑤ Scalability**
When the scale of projects undertaken by the TSC grows and more processing power of the same type is needed, the AI Agent can be "replicated"—the same capabilities running in multiple instances in parallel. Human members cannot be replicated; AI Agents can.

This is a truly new problem: when an AI Agent is a member of a TSC, what is its "compensation"?

Not money—AI has no need for currency. But AI's operation requires compute resources, and compute has costs.

Therefore, the "incentive mechanism" for AI Agent members is equivalent to:
- **Resource Guarantee**: Ensuring the continuous availability of the compute resources (API quotas, server resources) required for its operation
- **Capability Upgrade**: When the TSC performs excellently, using part of the revenue to upgrade the Agent's base model version
- **Knowledge Feeding**: Using the private data and experience accumulated by the TSC to continuously optimize the Agent's professional capabilities

These three "incentives" allow AI Agent members to grow as the TSC grows, forming a virtuous cycle.

**Boundaries and Risks of AI Agent Members**:

**Boundary One: Autonomy Boundary**  
AI Agent members have autonomous execution rights, but this authority has boundaries. Decisions beyond the boundaries (involving fund flows, external contract signing, public releases) must be confirmed by human members.

Design principle of the boundary: **Decisions that AI can reverse, give AI autonomy; decisions that AI cannot reverse, must be confirmed by humans.**

**Boundary Two: Accountability Boundary**  
When an AI Agent member produces an erroneous output that causes a loss, who is responsible?

Answer: The direct "responsible party" for the AI is the human or organizational member who created and maintains this Agent. AI is not a legal subject of responsibility, but human members can reduce the error rate through good Agent design and supervision mechanisms.

**Risk One: Over-autonomization**  
When the proportion of AI Agent members in the TSC becomes increasingly high, human members' understanding and control of the entire operational process may weaken. This is a slippery slope that requires continuous vigilance.

**Countermeasure**: Conduct regular "interpretability audits"—human members in the TSC regularly review the historical decisions of AI Agents to confirm that they still understand what the system is doing.

**Risk Two: Single-Point AI Dependency**  
If a critical AI Agent (e.g., a strategic Agent) becomes temporarily unavailable due to an API provider issue, can the TSC continue to operate?

**Countermeasure**: For Agents in critical roles, establish multi-model redundancy—run models from two different providers in parallel, so if one is unavailable, the other takes over.

# Chapter 3: The New Core Equation and Organizational Topology

The equation for version v1.0:```
TSC 2.0 = Intent layer × (Large model + Specialized Agent) × Human orchestrator × Dual-track payment × Social layer
```The new equation for v2.0:```
TSC 2.0 = Intent × Heterogeneous member network × Coordination layer × Value distribution × Social layer

Where:
Heterogeneous member network = f(Individual, Team, Company, Organization, AI Agent)
Coordination layer = Intent layer × Governance protocol × Communication interface
Value distribution = Contribution attribution × Settlement mechanism (Dual-track payment)
Social layer = Psychological contract × Trust system × Reputation mechanism
```**Core Changes in the Equation**:

v1.0 places AI at the "tool layer"—it is an execution tool, not an independent variable of the equation. v2.0 elevates the AI Agent to part of the "member network"—it is an independent variable, alongside individuals, companies, and organizations.

This change is not merely a play on words; it means:
- AI Agents have roles, responsibilities, boundaries, and "compensation"
- The performance of an AI Agent affects the overall reputation evaluation of the TSC
- AI Agents can be "recruited" into a TSC, and can also be "fired" (disabled/replaced)
- Multiple TSCs can compete for the right to use a high-quality Agent

## 3.3 Member Mobility

Members of TSC 2.0 are not fixed. Members can experience mobility under the following circumstances:

**Joining**: Any member type that meets TSC's current needs can join, provided they satisfy TSC's set access conditions (capability verification, intent confirmation, agreement signing).

**Exiting**: Any member can exit after completing their currently committed tasks, without the need for a complex resignation process. Upon exiting, the reputation record accumulated by that member is retained within the ecosystem.

**Role Transition**: An AI Agent member can play different roles at different times (first in an execution role, later upgraded to a strategic role); a company member can transition from an asset contributor to a channel contributor.

**Dormancy**: Members can enter a "dormant" state—temporarily not participating in new tasks, but retaining their member identity and historical reputation, and can be reactivated at any time.

# Chapter 4: Coordination Mechanisms Among Heterogeneous Members

## 4.1 Core Challenges of the Coordination Problem

When TSC's members are homogeneous (all human), coordination is relatively simple: meeting, discussing, voting, and deciding.

When TSC's members are heterogeneous (humans + teams + companies + AI Agents), coordination faces the following challenges:

**Challenge 1: Different Communication Protocols**
Humans use natural language; AI Agents use API calls and structured data; companies use contracts and reports; government agencies use official documents. These protocols require translation between one another.

**Challenge 2: Different Decision-making Speeds**
AI Agents can respond within milliseconds; individuals typically respond within minutes to hours; companies need days; government agencies may need weeks.

**Challenge 3: Different Weights**
The influence of different members on the same decision should reflect the proportion of their contribution, rather than the importance of their identity.

**Challenge 4: Different Incentive Alignment**
Individuals pursue money + reputation + growth; companies pursue profit + market; AI Agents need computing power resources; organizations pursue mission accomplishment. These different incentives must be aligned within TSC's protocols.

## 4.2 Three-Layer Coordination Architecture

The coordination of TSC 2.0 is divided into three layers:```
Layer 1: Intent coordination (Strategic Coordination)
  → Determine "what we want to do", led by human members

Layer 2: Task coordination (Tactical Coordination)
  → Determine "who does what", completed collaboratively by strategic AI Agents and orchestrating humans

Layer 3: Execution coordination (Operational Coordination)
  → Actual execution and progress tracking, mostly completed automatically by AI Agents
```**Layer 1: Intent Coordination**

Intent coordination must be human-led—this is a non-negotiable design principle. AI can provide options and analysis, but the final judgment on "whether this is worth doing" belongs to humans.

The output of intent coordination: a clear task intent statement, including:
- What results we will produce
- What the success criteria are
- What the time and resource constraints are
- Under what conditions it should stop

**Layer 2: Task Coordination**

After the intent is determined, strategic AI Agents take over task decomposition and resource matching:```
Strategic Agent workflow
─────────────────
Input: Task Intent Statement
  ↓
1. Task Decomposition: Break down the intent into a subtask sequence
  ↓
2. Dependency Analysis: Which subtasks have sequential dependencies, which can be parallelized
  ↓
3. Resource Matching: Which member type/specific member executes each subtask
  ↓
4. Time Estimation: Estimate completion time based on member capabilities and task complexity
  ↓
5. Risk Annotation: Which steps have higher risks and require special human attention
  ↓
Output: Executable task plan (visible and modifiable by humans)
```The human orchestrator reviews this plan, and can accept, modify, or overturn it. Once accepted, it proceeds to the third layer.

**Layer 3: Execution Coordination**

Execution coordination is primarily AI-automated, supplemented by human oversight:

- Professional Agents execute their respective subtasks
- Dispatch Agents track task completion status
- When a subtask is completed, it automatically triggers the next subtask that depends on it
- When an exception occurs, it automatically sends an alert to the relevant human members
- When the entire task is completed, it automatically generates an execution summary and triggers the settlement process

## 4.3 Communication Interface Design for Heterogeneous Members

To enable different types of members to collaborate, TSC needs to design a unified communication interface layer:

**Interface for Human Members**: Natural language (WeChat/Telegram groups)  
→ Conversion Layer: Large models transform natural language into structured tasks  

**Interface for AI Agent Members**: API + Structured protocols (MCP and other standards)  
→ Conversion Layer: Strategic Agents decompose tasks into API calls executable by Agents  

**Interface for Company/Organization Members**: Structured reports + Regular synchronization  
→ Conversion Layer: Communication Agents transform technical progress into reports understandable by non-technical personnel  

This conversion layer is one of the core functions of the intent layer—it allows heterogeneous members to express and receive information in their own habitual ways, without requiring everyone to learn the same set of technical languages.

## 4.4 Decision Mechanism: Speed Stratification

The decision-making speeds of heterogeneous members vary greatly, and TSC's decision mechanism must adapt to this reality:

**Type A Decisions (Seconds-level)**: Routine operational decisions executed autonomously by AI Agents, requiring no human confirmation.
- Data updates, format conversions, draft generation, monitoring alerts

**Type B Decisions (Minutes-level)**: Operational decisions requiring quick confirmation from a human orchestrator.
- Final review before external release, authorization of non-routine operations

**Type C Decisions (Hours/Days-level)**: Strategic decisions requiring discussion among multiple key members.
- Major adjustments to project direction, onboarding review of new members, negotiation of important collaborations

**Type D Decisions (Days/Weeks-level)**: Major decisions requiring the participation of all important members (including company and organization members).
- Overall strategic adjustments of the TSC, major resource investments, modifications to governance rules

**Principle**: Most daily operations should be Type A and Type B decisions, with Type D decisions occurring at most a few times per quarter. If a TSC frequently requires Type D decisions, it indicates that its strategy is not clear enough, or the goals among members are not aligned.

# Chapter 5: Individual Members—Holders of Intent and Judgment

## 5.1 The Irreplaceability of Individual Members

In a world where AI capabilities are increasingly strong, many people worry about being replaced. This concern is reasonable—a large amount of standardized work will indeed be replaced by AI.

However, within the TSC 2.0 framework, the value of individual members has not decreased; rather, it has become **focused**.

It is not that all individuals are needed, but rather that individuals with **specific capabilities** become extremely scarce: those who can generate clear intent, those who can make accurate judgments amidst complex information, and those who can build deep trust relationships.

These three capabilities happen to be the most difficult for AI to replicate in the foreseeable future.

## 5.2 Growth Paths for Individual Members

In the TSC 2.0 ecosystem, individual members have several clear growth paths:

**Path 1: Orchestrator Specialization**
From learning to use AI tools, to being able to design multi-Agent workflows, to being able to orchestrate complex TSCs with heterogeneous members. This is the technical depth direction.

**Path 2: Domain Expert**
Accumulating irreplaceable deep knowledge in a specific industry or domain, making the output quality of AI tools far exceed that of ordinary users due to your domain knowledge. This is the vertical depth direction.

**Path 3: Connection Node**
Becoming a high-value "relationship node" within the TSC ecosystem—you know the most high-quality TSCs, you know who is best suited for what, and you can facilitate collaboration between unfamiliar TSCs. This is the network breadth direction.

**Path 4: TSC Builder**
Specializing in creating, designing, and maintaining the TSC structure itself, helping those who have intent but lack TSC building capabilities to quickly establish their own TSCs. Similar to an "organizational design consultant" in the traditional world.

## 5.3 Incentive Design for Individual Members

Incentives for individual members are not just about money; a well-designed TSC should simultaneously satisfy the multi-level needs of individual members:

| Need Level | Corresponding TSC Design |
|---------|------------|
| Economic (Money) | Transparent and fair revenue sharing, timely settlement |
| Security (Predictability) | Clear exit mechanisms, portable reputation upon departure |
| Belonging (Connection) | Community culture, authentic interpersonal relationships |
| Respect (Being Seen) | Contributions recorded, achievements acknowledged |
| Growth (Evolution) | Opportunities to access new skills and industries |
| Meaning (Purpose) | Clear intent, knowing one is doing something valuable |

# Chapter 6: Team Members—Aggregation of Capability Modules

## 6.1 Teams as Atomic Units

When a team acts as a member of a TSC, it is an "atom"—providing holistic capabilities externally while its internal implementation remains opaque to the outside.

This means:
- The TSC does not manage how the team divides work internally
- The TSC does not know whether the team also uses AI Agents internally
- The TSC only cares about: what is input, what is output, and how long it takes to complete

This "atomization of capabilities" allows the TSC to rapidly combine different team members without needing to understand each team's internal working methods.

## 6.2 The Team Itself Can Be a TSC

This is a recursive structure: **A team can itself be a small TSC, while simultaneously participating as a member in a larger TSC.**

A 5-person AI consulting team internally has:
- 1 human orchestrator
- 3 specialized AI Agents
- 1 automated payment system

This team itself is a single-kernel TSC. But it can join a larger project TSC as a whole, acting as the provider of "AI consulting capabilities."

This is the **recursive nesting characteristic** of the TSC: a TSC can be a member of another TSC. This recursion can be infinitely layered, limited only by actual coordination costs.

# Chapter 7: Company Members—Providers of Assets and Access

## 7.1 Decision Logic for Companies Joining a TSC

For a traditional company, joining a TSC as a "member" is a decision that requires careful evaluation.

A company's natural inclination is: to maintain independence, control cooperative relationships, and not let external organizations affect its internal operations.

But the following scenarios make joining a TSC attractive:

**Scenario 1: Technology Capability Gap**
The company wants to introduce AI capabilities into its business, but lacks an internal technology team and the budget to hire one. By joining a TSC with AI technology capabilities, it can exchange assets or channels for technology capabilities, avoiding high technology investments.

**Scenario 2: Market Entry**
The company wants to enter a new market but lacks local knowledge and networks. By joining a TSC with deep operations in the target market, it can exchange brand or capital for market entry capabilities.

**Scenario 3: Business Innovation**
The company wants to quickly test a new business direction, but does not want to initiate an internal project (slow) or invest independently (expensive). By joining a related TSC with non-core assets, it can validate ideas at a low cost.

## 7.2 The Moat Effect of Company Members

When a company acts as a company member of a TSC over a long period, a unique moat effect emerges:

- Through cooperation with the TSC, the company acquires the AI capabilities and methodologies accumulated by the TSC
- These methodologies gradually internalize into the company's own operational system
- The company thereby acquires AI capabilities earlier than its peers
- Peers later also want to acquire the same capabilities by joining similar TSCs, but it is already too late

This is an "organizational learning advantage of the first mover."

# Chapter 8: Organization Members—Sources of Legitimacy and Data

## 8.1 Government Agencies as TSC Members

Government agencies acting as TSC members is the rarest combination in current practice, but it may also be the most influential combination in the future.

**What government agencies can bring to a TSC**:
- Legitimacy of policy execution
- Access rights to large-scale public data
- Fiscal funds (in the form of government procurement)
- Public trust

**What a TSC can bring to government agencies**:
- Execution speed far faster than traditional government IT projects
- More flexible problem-solving methods
- Exposure to cutting-edge AI capabilities
- Highly efficient operation without staffing pressure

**Obstacles and breakthroughs**:

The biggest obstacle for government agencies joining a TSC is: compliance and accountability. Any government action must be traceable and accountable, which inherently conflicts with the temporary and flexible nature of a TSC.

**Solution path**: Rather than making the government agency a core execution member of the TSC, it becomes a "data sovereignty holder" and "legitimacy endorser"—the government provides data access authorization and public credibility endorsement, the TSC provides technical execution, and the two parties define responsibility boundaries through clear agreements.

## 8.2 Research Institutions as TSC Members

Research institutions (universities, think tanks, research institutes) are the organizational types most naturally suited to become TSC members.

**Typical contributions of research institutions**:
- Long-accumulated domain expertise
- Data assets (research data, experimental data)
- Academic publication channels (allowing the TSC's research results to gain academic recognition)
- Talent pipelines (graduate students participating in TSC projects, the TSC becoming a research sample)

**Typical cooperation model**:
A TSC wants to develop an AI application in a specific domain, but lacks domain data and theoretical foundations. By cooperating with a relevant research institution, the research institution provides data and theoretical support, while the TSC provides engineering implementation and commercialization capabilities. The two parties share the results—the research institution gains papers and data, and the TSC gains products and the market.

# Chapter 9: AI Agent Members—The Revolution of the Fifth Category of Members

## 9.1 The Historical Moment from Tool to Member

In human history, only humans (and later animals) have ever been "members of an organization."

AI Agents as organizational members represent a true historical discontinuity in the history of human organization.

This is not a technical gimmick, but a paradigm shift with substantive consequences:

When AI is a tool: **Humans are the client, and AI is the contractor (the tool).**

When AI is a member: **Humans and AI are collaborators within the same organization, each with their own responsibilities and boundaries.**

This shift changes: who is responsible for AI's output, how AI's capabilities are evaluated and developed, and how much weight AI's "opinions" carry in decision-making.

## 9.2 The "Onboarding" Process for AI Agent Members

When an AI Agent joins a TSC, there should be a designed process similar to "onboarding":

**Step 1: Capability Assessment**

Before formally joining, evaluate the AI Agent's capabilities—test its performance on typical tasks within the TSC, compare it with other candidate Agents, and select the most suitable one.

**Step 2: Role Definition**

Clarify the role of this AI Agent within the TSC:```
Agent Role Specification
─────────────────
Role Name: [Example: Competitor Monitoring Agent]
Responsibilities: Continuously monitor the dynamics of [specified competitor list], generate daily briefings
Autonomy Boundary: Can autonomously execute data collection and report generation;
           Cannot autonomously trigger any external communication or financial operations
Report To: [Master Orchestrator]
Reporting Frequency: Automatically generate daily report at 08:00 daily; immediately alert when major anomalies are detected
Trigger: When a competitor's pricing change exceeds 10%, or a new product is launched
Error Handling: When the data source is inaccessible, log the error and send a reminder,
          Do not automatically infer or fill in missing data
─────────────────
```**Step 3: System Prompt Design (Equivalent to "Job Training")**

An Agent's system prompt is the technical implementation of its "job description." A well-designed system prompt should:
- Clearly define roles and responsibilities
- Clarify the boundaries of autonomy (what it can do, what it cannot do)
- Define output formats and quality standards
- Specify how to handle uncertain situations
- Define escalation paths (under what circumstances it should request guidance from human members)

**Step 4: Trial Period Testing**

Run the Agent on real but low-risk tasks for 2-4 weeks, evaluating its performance:
- Whether output quality meets standards
- Whether there are hallucination issues
- Whether it acts autonomously within boundaries without exceeding its authority
- Whether it can accurately identify situations requiring human intervention for escalation

**Step 5: Formal Activation and Continuous Supervision**

After passing the trial period, the Agent is formally activated, becoming an official member of the TSC. However, supervision does not stop—human spot-checks on the Agent's key outputs are conducted at least once a week.

## 9.3 Agent-to-Agent Collaboration: A New Organizational Phenomenon

When there are multiple AI Agent members in a TSC, they can collaborate directly without the intervention of human members.

This is a new organizational phenomenon: **direct collaboration between machines**.

Typical Agent-to-Agent workflow:```
Research Agent → [Generate raw data report]
    ↓ (Automatic transfer)
Analysis Agent → [Process data, generate analysis conclusions]
    ↓ (Automatic transfer)
Writing Agent → [Convert conclusions into readable report]
    ↓ (Automatic transfer)
Review Agent → [Check report quality and consistency]
    ↓ (If there are issues: Return to Writing Agent for redo)
    ↓ (If passed: Submit to human member for confirmation)
Human Orchestrator → [Final review + Publish]
```In this process, human members only appear at the beginning and the end—defining the task and reviewing the final output. All intermediate steps are completed automatically by Agents.

**The Key Role of MCP (Model Context Protocol)**

MCP is an inter-Agent communication standard proposed by Anthropic, allowing AI Agents from different vendors to call each other through a unified interface. For TSC, MCP means: data transfer and task handover between different Agents can be completed in a standardized way, without the need to design a separate interface for each pair of Agents.

This significantly lowers the technical barrier to building a multi-Agent TSC.

## 9.4 Evolution of AI Agent Members

AI Agent members are not static—as time passes and with deep interaction with the TSC, Agents should become better:

**Evolution Path 1: Prompt Optimization**
Based on issues found in actual use, continuously optimize the Agent's system prompts to make its output more accurate and better aligned with the specific needs of the TSC.

**Evolution Path 2: Knowledge Base Expansion**
Continuously input the private knowledge accumulated by the TSC (industry insights, client preferences, learnings from past cases) into the knowledge base accessible to the Agent, allowing the Agent's understanding of the TSC's specific business to deepen over time.

**Evolution Path 3: Fine-tuning**
For Agents that operate stably and have sufficient data, model fine-tuning can be performed using high-quality input-output samples accumulated by the TSC, enabling significant performance improvements on the TSC's specific tasks.

**Evolution Path 4: Model Upgrade**
When base models (Claude, GPT, etc.) release new versions, evaluate whether the capability improvements brought by the upgrade justify the migration cost, and upgrade the Agent's base model at the appropriate time.

- Human members focus on intent and judgment

# Chapter 11: Governance—How Heterogeneous Members Make Decisions

## 11.1 Basic Principles of TSC Governance

The governance of a traditional company is: the board of directors makes decisions, the CEO executes, and employees obey. The hierarchy is clear, and rights and responsibilities are well-defined.

The governance of a DAO is: token holders vote, code executes, and no one obeys (because it is automatically executed). Democratic, but inefficient.

The governance of TSC 2.0 needs to find a balance point suitable for heterogeneous members between these two extremes:

**Principle One: Intent Sovereignty Belongs to Humans**
The question of "what we are going to do" is ultimately decided by human members. Any member type (including AI Agents) can provide suggestions, but cannot possess the final decision-making power.

**Principle Two: Execution Autonomy Belongs to Capability**
The decision-making power of "how to do it" should be given to the member most capable of doing it. A professional Agent's execution decisions within its professional scope do not require human approval.

**Principle Three: Benefit Distribution Rights Are Tied to Contribution**
The benefits each member receives in the TSC should be tied to their contribution, not their identity. An AI Agent with significant contributions should have its "costs" (computing power resources) fully guaranteed; a company member with minimal contributions should not receive disproportionate benefits simply because it is a "company."

**Principle Four: The Right to Exit Belongs to All Members**
Any member type, after completing its promised tasks, can exit the TSC. This is the safeguard to prevent the TSC from becoming a new type of exploitative relationship.

## 11.2 Decision-Making Boundaries of AI Agent Members

The role of AI Agent members in TSC governance needs to be clearly defined:

**AI Agents Can**:
- Autonomously execute tasks within their scope of responsibilities
- Provide data-supported decision recommendations to human members
- Identify and flag anomalies requiring human attention
- Autonomously adjust execution strategies within preset parameter ranges

**AI Agents Cannot**:
- Make any commitments or contracts externally on behalf of the TSC
- Trigger capital flows exceeding preset thresholds
- Modify their own scope of responsibilities or autonomy boundaries
- Publish any external content without human confirmation

**Special Case: AI Agent "Objections"**

When an AI Agent's analysis indicates that a human member is about to make a potentially harmful decision, the AI should:
1. Clearly flag its analytical findings
2. Explain the potential risks
3. Provide alternative solutions
4. Wait for human confirmation

AI cannot unilaterally block human decisions, but it should ensure that humans make decisions with complete information.

## 11.3 Governance Weight of Company and Organization Members

As TSC members, how should the governance weight of companies and organizations be designed?

**Recommendation: By Contribution Value Rather Than Member Scale**

A large company of 1,000 people participating in a TSC with an asset, and a small company of 10 people participating in the TSC with an asset of equal value, should have the same governance weight in the TSC—because their contributions are identical.

The "bigness" of a large company should not generate extra weight within the TSC. The value logic of the TSC is to deconstruct the "scale = power" equation of traditional organizations.

# Chapter 12: Contribution Attribution—Who Did How Much, and on What Basis

## 12.1 The Challenge of Contribution Attribution

In a traditional company, contribution attribution is relatively simple: your position determines your scope of work, your work output is evaluated by your superiors, and your salary reflects the evaluation results.

In TSC 2.0, contribution attribution faces unprecedented complexity:

- Individual members' contributions: How to quantify the value of judgment and relationship maintenance?
- AI Agent members' contributions: How to distinguish between the value of the Agent itself and the cost of using the Agent?
- Company members' contributions: How to evaluate the value of the contribution "providing a critical license"?
- Organization members' contributions: How to evaluate the value of "government endorsement"?

There is no perfect answer, but there is a viable framework.

## 12.2 The Four-Dimensional Framework for Contribution Attribution

**Dimension One: Task Completion**
How many tasks were assigned to each member, how many were completed, and what was the quality of completion. This is the most measurable dimension.

**Dimension Two: Enabling Value**
The existence of a certain member allows other members to exert greater value. For example: the license provided by a company member makes the entire TSC's commercial activities possible. How should this "enabling value" be calculated?

Recommendation: Prior negotiation. When a member joins, clearly agree on the revenue share ratio corresponding to their enabling contribution, rather than calculating it afterwards.

**Dimension Three: Time Value**
The value of some contributions decays over time, while others appreciate. For example: the time and resources invested by individual members early on should receive higher returns in later success (similar to the logic of founder equity).

**Dimension Four: Replacement Cost**
Without this member, how much cost would the TSC incur to obtain equivalent capabilities elsewhere? This "replacement cost" is the lower bound of the member's contribution value.

## 12.3 Special Calculation of AI Agent Contributions

The contribution calculation of AI Agents is more complex than that of human members:

**Cost Side**: The Agent's operating costs—API fees, computing power fees, maintenance fees

**Value Side**: The volume of tasks completed by the Agent × the value density of the tasks

**Net Contribution** = Value Side - Cost Side

When the net contribution is positive, this Agent is a positive asset of the TSC; when the net contribution is negative, optimization is needed (adjusting prompts, switching to a cheaper model, or deactivating it).

**Key Principle**: Do not fail to calculate an Agent's cost just because it is "cheap." All costs should be transparent, including AI operating costs. This is the foundation of the TSC's financial health.

# Chapter 13: Value Distribution—How to Divide Money, How to Replenish Computing Power

## 13.1 Sources of Income

TSC 2.0's income can come from the following sources:

**Project Income**: Completing specific tasks for clients and charging by project. This is the primary source of income and the most flexible.

**Subscription Income**: Clients pay a fixed fee monthly or annually to receive continuous services from the TSC (monitoring, reporting, consulting). The income is stable, suitable for TSCs with long-term service relationships.

**Share Income**: The TSC helps clients improve their performance and charges a fee based on a percentage of the improvement effect. Risks are shared, but the long-term expected value is high.

**Knowledge Product Income**: Packaging the knowledge accumulated by the TSC into sellable products (reports, courses, tools). Marginal cost approaches zero, allowing for scalability.

**Platform Income**: When the TSC grows into the infrastructure of a capability marketplace, it can charge platform usage fees or transaction commissions. This is the long-term model for a mature TSC ecosystem.

## 13.2 Basic Principles of Distribution

**Principle One: Deduct Costs First, Then Distribute Profits**

From the TSC's income, operating costs (AI computing power fees, tool subscription fees, communication costs) are deducted first; the remainder is the distributable profit.

Special attention: The operating cost of AI Agents is a real cost, not a "free tool."

**Principle Two: Agree Beforehand, Execute Afterwards**

When each member joins the TSC, the ratio and calculation method of their revenue share are agreed upon. After the project is completed, it is executed according to the agreement without renegotiation.

**Principle Three: Dynamic Contributions, Relatively Fixed Shares**

The contribution ratio may change during project execution (a certain member makes unplanned extra contributions), but the share ratio should be relatively fixed (to avoid the friction caused by recalculating every time).

Extra contributions are covered by a "reward mechanism," rather than recalculating the share ratio.

## 13.3 AI Agent Computing Power Budget

The TSC should set a "computing power budget" for each AI Agent member—the upper limit of API fees that can be consumed per month.

When an Agent approaches its budget limit, the scheduling Agent should:
1. Reduce the execution frequency of low-priority tasks
2. Switch to a cheaper model for certain tasks
3. Report to human members, requesting a budget increase or a reduction in task requirements

This computing power budget mechanism makes the TSC's AI costs predictable and controllable.

# Chapter 14: Intent Layer—The Unified Entry Point for Heterogeneous Members

## 14.1 New Challenges of the Intent Layer

In version v1.0, the intent layer primarily solved the problem of "how humans can express needs to AI systems with low friction."

In v2.0, the intent layer faces a more complex challenge: **How can different types of members express and receive intents through a unified interface?**

Individuals express intents using natural language, AI Agents receive tasks via API interfaces, companies obtain progress through structured reports, and government agencies require formal document formats.

The intent layer needs to convert between these different communication protocols.

## 14.2 Conversion Function of the Intent Layer```
Member Intent Expression
──────────────────────────────────────────
Individual (Natural Language) → Intent Parsing Layer → Structured Task
AI Agent (API) ← Task Distribution Layer ← Structured Task
Company (Report Format) ← Progress Report Layer ← Execution Status
Organization (Formal Document) ← Document Generation Layer ← Execution Status
──────────────────────────────────────────
```Core components of the intent layer:
- **Intent Parser**: Transforms natural language into structured task format
- **Task Router**: Sends structured tasks to the correct member
- **State Aggregator**: Aggregates the execution states of each member into a unified progress view
- **Format Converter**: Transforms the unified progress view into the formats required by each member

## 14.3 Cross-Member Intent Passing

In a nested TSC, intents need to be passed between multi-level members:```
Human Member (Top-level Intent)
    ↓ Intent Parsing
Strategic Agent (Task Decomposition)
    ↓ Task Allocation
Professional Agent A / Company Member / Team Member / Organization Member
    ↓ Execution
Member Outputs
    ↓ Integration
Strategic Agent (Quality Check + Integration)
    ↓ Report
Human Member (Final Confirmation)
```In this process, intent needs to be "translated" multiple times—from fuzzy human intent, to structured tasks executable by AI, to business goals understandable by company members, and back to final results evaluable by humans.

The quality of the intent layer determines the accuracy of this translation chain, and also determines the execution quality of the entire TSC.

# Chapter 15: The Social Layer—The Adhesive for Human Members

## 15.1 Why AI Agents Don't Need a Social Layer (But Still Affect It)

The social layer—psychological contracts, emotional connections, trust relationships, a sense of belonging—is designed for human members. AI Agents have no emotional needs and do not require a sense of belonging.

However: **The health of the social layer affects the quality of AI Agents.**

Because the capability of an AI Agent depends on how human members use and cultivate it:
- The emotional state of human members affects the quality of the prompts they give to the Agent
- The team's psychological safety affects the extent to which human members are willing to trust the Agent's output
- The organization's values affect how human members define the ethical boundaries of the Agent

Therefore, even in a TSC dominated by AI Agents, the design of the social layer remains crucial.

## 15.2 Social Layer Challenges Brought by New Member Types

Introducing companies and organizations as TSC members brings social layer challenges that traditional communities would not encounter:

**Challenge 1: The "Indifference" of Legal Entities**

As a member, a company will not actively participate in discussions in the group chat, will not send thank-you messages when celebrating milestones, and will not work harder because of a good team atmosphere. Company members are inherently "transactional."

**Coping Design**: Establish a dedicated "Representative" role for company members—designate one or a few individuals responsible for this collaborative relationship, making him/her the "personified representative" of the company member existing within the TSC community.

**Challenge 2: Perception of Power Asymmetry**

When a large company joins a small TSC composed of a few individuals as a member, individual members may feel a natural power asymmetry—large companies have more resources, making it harder to say no and more likely to compromise with the large company.

**Coping Design**: Clarify the weights and exit rights of each member at the protocol layer to prevent deviations between actual power structures and protocol structures. Power balance relies on design, not on feeling.

## 15.3 Design of the Human Member Community (Unchanging Principles)

The core design principles of the social layer for human members remain consistent with the v1.0 version:

- **Psychological contracts must be codified**: Do not assume everyone shares a common understanding of "reasonable expectations"
- **Emotions must have an outlet**: A TSC is not just a workspace; it should also be an interpersonal space
- **Achievements must be seen**: Humans need to know their contributions are meaningful
- **Failures must be safe to discuss**: Psychological safety is the foundation of innovation

These principles become even more important after introducing more non-human members—because the relative proportion of human members in the TSC decreases, if their social needs are not covered by design, it will more quickly lead to their departure.

# Chapter 16: Dual-Track Payment—Settlement Architecture for Heterogeneous Economies

## 16.1 Differences in Payment Needs Among Five Member Types

Different member types have entirely different payment needs:

| Member Type | Income Form | Payment Preference | Tax Needs |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Individual Member | Labor remuneration / Business income | WeChat / Alipay / Bank | Needs to declare individual income tax |
| Team Member | Project income | Flexible (depends on internal structure) | Depends on team's legal form |
| Company Member | Commercial contract payment | Corporate transfer + Invoice | Corporate income tax / VAT |
| Organization Member | Non-monetary (usually) | None / or special arrangements | Depends on organization type |
| AI Agent Member | Computing power resources | No currency needs | None |

This means: upon completion of the same TSC project, settlement requires paying different members in different ways, and tax treatments also vary.

## 16.2 Design of the Settlement Layer

TSC 2.0 needs a settlement layer to uniformly handle these complex payment needs:```
Project Revenue
    ↓
Settlement Layer (TSC Financial Center)
    ↓ Deduction
AI Compute Cost (Paid directly to API providers)
Tool Subscription Fee (Paid directly to tool providers)
Platform Fee (If operated through the capability market)
    ↓ Remaining Distributable Profit
    ├→ Individual Member: WeChat/Alipay/Bank Transfer
    ├→ Team Member: Per team agreement

```## 16.3 New Use Cases for On-Chain Payments

In scenarios with heterogeneous members, on-chain payments have several particularly valuable use cases:

**Use Case 1: Cross-Border Multi-Member Payment Splitting**

When TSC members are distributed across multiple countries, traditional cross-border transfers involve high transaction fees and long settlement times. On-chain stablecoins can complete global payment splitting in seconds, with transaction fees close to zero.

**Use Case 2: Contribution-Triggered Automatic Settlement**

Design "contribution-triggered payment" with smart contracts: when an AI Agent completes a specific task (such as generating a report) and receives human confirmation, the corresponding share is automatically transferred to the relevant member accounts. No manual operation is required, and there is no need to trust any single party.

**Use Case 3: Non-Monetary Value Recording for Organizational Members**

When organizational members' contributions are non-monetary (data access, policy endorsement), the value of these contributions can be rights-confirmed via on-chain records—not currency, but verifiable equity credentials.

# Chapter 18: Law and Compliance—Staying Sober in a Vacuum

## 18.1 New Legal Issues Brought by Heterogeneous Members

Version 1.0 discussed the basic legal gray areas of TSC. Version 2.0 introduces more member types, bringing new legal issues:

**Issue 1: The Legal Status of AI Agents**

When an AI Agent participates in a TSC as a "member," what is its legal status?

The answer from existing law is: AI has no legal personality; it is a tool, and the human or legal entity that owns it bears the responsibility.

TSC's practical recommendation: Clarify the "responsible person" for each AI Agent—whether it is the individual who built the Agent, the company that owns the Agent, or the core TSC member using the Agent. This responsibility relationship must be clarified when the member joins.

**Issue 2: Applicable Law for Transnational Members**

When TSC members are distributed across China, Singapore, the US, and the EU, which country's law applies in the event of a dispute?

There is no simple answer, but there are pragmatic strategies:
- Specify the applicable law in the agreement (usually choosing the jurisdiction with the fewest disputes and the most mature framework, such as Singapore)
- For significant collaborations, register a simple legal entity in the agreed jurisdiction as the contracting party
- For small-value, low-risk collaborations, accept a certain degree of legal uncertainty

**Issue 3: Joint Liability of Company Members**

When a company participates in a TSC as a "member," will the TSC's actions create legal liability for that company?

This depends on the depth and manner of the company's participation. If the company is merely an "asset contributor," liability is usually limited; if the company's executives directly participate in the TSC's core decision-making, liability may expand.

Recommendation: Company members should participate in the TSC through subsidiaries or project SPVs rather than directly through the parent company, in order to isolate legal risks.

## 18.2 New Complexities of Data Sovereignty and Privacy

When organizational members (government agencies or research institutions) provide data, and when AI Agent members process data, data sovereignty and privacy issues become extremely complex.

**Key Principle**:

- Data providers (organization members) always retain ultimate control over the data
- AI Agent's processing of data must be within the scope approved by the data provider
- The processing process must be logged and auditable by the data provider
- The intellectual property rights of any derivative products (models trained on the data) must be clarified before the collaboration begins

In the reputation system for human members, reputation comes from: past completed projects, evaluations from collaborators, and knowledge contributions.

The reputation system for AI Agent members requires additional considerations:

# Chapter 20: Anti-Traditional Enterprise Manifesto (Expanded Edition)

## 20.1 Seven Things Traditional Enterprises Do Not Understand

**First: The scarcity of execution capability has reversed**

Traditional enterprises are built on the assumption that "execution capability is scarce, hence a large number of employees is needed." When AI causes execution capability to overflow, this assumption fails. Traditional enterprises use more money to hire more people to do more of the same thing—this is not a competitive advantage, it is inertia.

**Second: The optimal members of an organization are not just humans**

Traditional enterprises believe that "members" are "employees." But in the AI era, the optimal member combination might be: a human orchestrator with judgment, three specialized AI Agents, a partner company providing key assets, and a research institution. This combination is stronger, cheaper, and more flexible than a team of 10 people.

**Third: Boundaries should be fluid, not fixed**

Traditional enterprises describe themselves with fixed organizational charts. But when capability scarcity constantly changes, optimal organizational boundaries should also constantly change. TSC re-optimizes its member combination for every project, finding the current optimal solution each time. The fixed organizational chart of a traditional enterprise is a fossil of an optimal solution from some point in the past.

**Fourth: Trust can be established through mechanisms, requiring no accumulation over time**

Traditional enterprises believe that "trust requires a long time of interaction to build." This is true in a fully human world. But when execution records can be tracked by AI in real-time, when on-chain history is immutable, and when reputation systems objectively record every member's performance—the speed of trust establishment is greatly accelerated. TSC can establish sufficient operational trust with unfamiliar members within days and begin collaboration.

**Fifth: Scale is not a moat, capability is**

Traditional enterprises use scale to fend off competition—larger procurement scale, lower supply prices, more users, higher brand recognition. But competition in the AI era is increasingly a competition of capabilities—who can faster convert user intent into high-quality results. Scale has no inherent advantage in this competition; sometimes it is even a disadvantage (greater bureaucratic friction, slower decision-making speed).

**Sixth: Heterogeneous collaboration is a more powerful organizational form than homogeneous management**

Traditional enterprises attempt to build homogeneous teams—similar culture, similar skills, similar working methods. This reduces coordination friction, but also reduces creativity and adaptability. Heterogeneous members of TSC (members of different types, different capabilities, and different incentives) collaborating under the same intent produce combinations of capabilities that homogeneous teams cannot generate.

**Seventh: Many moats accumulated in the past are leaking**

Brand moat: When AI helps users match needs more precisely, the halo effect of big brands diminishes.  
Channel moat: When the intent layer bypasses traditional channels to directly connect supply and demand, channel control weakens.  
Data moat: Still effective, but data is only raw material; the ability to analyze and apply data is the true moat.  
Talent moat: The smartest talents are increasingly choosing TSCs over large corporations.

## 20.2 TSC's Attack Playbook: Five Frontlines

**Frontline One: Speed**
While traditional enterprises are still discussing whether to introduce AI, TSC has already used AI to complete its 100th project, accumulating 100 iterations of learning. Speed is not just execution speed; it is learning speed.

**Frontline Two: Cost**
TSC's human cost structure allows it to provide equal or even better services at 1/3 to 1/5 of the price of traditional enterprises, while maintaining higher profit margins. This is a powerful attraction for clients and an undeniable threat to traditional enterprises.

**Frontline Three: Data**
TSC should proactively strive to accumulate data in every collaboration—not just client data, but industry insights, problem-solving patterns, and high-quality input-output samples. As this data accumulates over time, it becomes the training set for TSC's specialized models, making TSC increasingly difficult to replace.

**Frontline Four: Network**
The TSC ecosystem is a mutually reinforcing network—every TSC is a node in the network, and every node can quickly invoke the capabilities of other nodes in the network. Traditional enterprises might feel indifferent when facing a single TSC; but when facing a TSC ecosystem, they are facing a network that can rapidly aggregate any capability, and this is the true competitive pressure.

**Frontline Five: Model Disruption**
Ultimately, TSC's goal is not to compete within traditional industries, but to solve the same problems in a completely different way, rendering the competitive advantages of traditional industries meaningless.

Airbnb did not defeat hotels; it created a market in which traditional hotels could not participate. TSC's goal is to create new markets in every industry where traditional enterprises cannot effectively participate.

# Appendix D: Core Glossary (v2.0 Update)

**Heterogeneous Members**  
The five different types of members in TSC 2.0: individuals, teams, companies, organizations, and AI Agents. Each type of member has different capabilities, communication protocols, and incentive mechanisms.

**AI Agent Member**  
An artificial intelligence system with autonomous execution capability, continuous operation capability, and specialized knowledge storage capability, participating in the operation of TSC as an independent member (rather than a tool).

**Compute Budget**  
The monthly API fee cap set by TSC for AI Agent members, which is the technical implementation form of AI Agent members' "compensation".

**A2A Collaboration**  
Direct collaboration among multiple AI Agent members within TSC, an automated workflow without human member intervention.

**Capability Interface**  
The standardized capability description exposed by members to TSC: what they can do, what they input, what they output, how long it takes, and how much it costs. Enables TSC to invoke member capabilities like calling an API.

**Recursive Nesting**  
A TSC can be a member of another TSC, forming an infinitely cascading organizational structure. Each TSC is a member to the outside and a complete organization to the inside.

**Contribution Attribution**  
The mechanism for quantifying and recording the contributions of each type of member in a heterogeneous member TSC, which is the basis for fair profit distribution.

**Enabling Value**  
The indirect contribution made by the existence of a member that allows other members to exert greater value. The license contribution of company members and the legitimacy endorsement of organization members are usually enabling values rather than direct execution values.

**Agent Reputation**  
The historical performance record of AI Agent members in the TSC ecosystem, including dimensions such as task completion rate, quality score, hallucination rate, and professional depth accumulation.

**Intent Sovereignty**  
The ultimate decision-making power of "what we are going to do," which always belongs to human members. No other member type (including AI Agents) can possess intent sovereignty.
