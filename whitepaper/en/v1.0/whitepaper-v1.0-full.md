# Preface: Why Now

Late one night in November 2025, an Austrian developer spent an hour connecting WhatsApp's interface with Claude's API. Within 72 hours, his project OpenClaw had garnered 60,000 GitHub Stars. Four months later, it exceeded 250,000 Stars, becoming the fastest-growing open-source project in GitHub history. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang said this might be the most important software release ever.

There is a detail in this incident that most people overlooked: he thought it was too simple, and that OpenAI or Anthropic must have already done it. They hadn't.

Why not? It wasn't a technical issue. It was an organizational structure issue.

Large companies cannot do this kind of thing, not because they aren't smart enough, but because the structure itself generates friction. Meetings, approvals, KPIs, departmental silos—while these things keep the company running "stably," they also strip the company of the ability that an individual could accomplish in just one hour.

This is a microcosm of the structural problems of traditional enterprises.

Meanwhile, another reality is rapidly taking shape: Midjourney, 11 people, $200 million in annual revenue; Cursor, 20 people, $100 million in annual revenue; Lovable, 50 people, taking less than a year to go from 0 to $100 million in annual revenue. The revenue per employee of these companies is 6 to 20 times that of traditional software companies.

This is not a technological miracle. This is the triumph of organizational form.

But these companies are still stuck in the form of "product companies." No one has truly combined the elements of "composable at any time, nestable at any time, intent-as-activation, on-chain settleable, community-as-organization" to form a replicable organizational primitive.

This is what the Thin-Shell Company 2.0 whitepaper sets out to do.

This is not an academic paper, not an investment pitch deck, nor an inspirational book. It is an operating manual, and also a manifesto. Its target readers are those who already feel the turning of the era and want to find their place within this rotation.

You might be an independent developer, a founder of a small team, a middle manager in a large company feeling suffocated by the existing structure, or a young person just entering the industry wanting to figure out the rules of the game in this era.

The core proposition of this whitepaper is very simple:

**The most competitive organizations of the future will not be larger companies, but smarter structures.**

When traditional enterprises employ people, they consume enormous friction costs in managing, motivating, and retaining them. The most competitive organizational form in the AI era is one that reduces this friction cost to near zero—using large models and specialized Agents to undertake the execution layer, humans to undertake the judgment and intent layers, communities to replace hierarchies, and on-chain settlement to replace finance departments.

We call this organizational form the Thin-Shell Company 2.0 (TSC 2.0).

It has pioneers, but no complete precedents. Its time is now.

# Chapter 1: The Era Fault Line—Where We Stand

## 1.1 What Happened in Those Five Years

To understand why the Thin-Shell Company 2.0 is possible, one must first understand what actually happened in these five years. Not just a vague "AI is very strong," but looking at specific numbers, because the shape of these numbers has changed the underlying logic of the world.

**2021**: The world's most advanced AI scored 35 on GSM8K (a grade school math word problem test set with 8,500 problems). This is not a joke; this is real test data, the actual performance of the best model of that era.

**2026**: Same type of test, score of 99. Five years.

The shape of this curve is not a slow climb; it is a vertical line.

Then the test creators felt embarrassed, as the test had lost its meaning, so they created harder problems.

Hundreds of the world's top experts joined forces to create an exam that "AI would absolutely fail," called Humanity's Last Exam (HLE). The design rule was: as long as an existing AI could answer a question correctly, that question would be replaced, until AI completely failed to answer. When this exam came out at the end of 2024, the top AI's score was in the single-digit percentages.

By February 2026, Claude Opus 4's score was 53.1%.

From single digits to over half, it took about a year and a half.

The SWE-bench test, specifically designed to evaluate whether AI can solve real code bugs, saw the strongest model score 80.8% in early 2026—randomly selecting 100 real bugs, the AI could independently handle over 80 of them. This number was less than 5% in early 2024.

These are not advances in a specific domain, but a leap in **general reasoning ability**. This means that for almost all tasks with clear inputs and outputs, that are verifiable, and that can be structurally described, AI is rapidly acquiring the ability to execute them.

## 1.2 The Shape of the Capability Curve Changed Everything

Why is the "shape of the curve" so important?

Because the design of human organizations is almost entirely predicated on the assumption that "execution capability is scarce." Factories need many workers because physical execution is scarce. Companies need many clerks because information processing is scarce. Companies need many middle managers because coordinating execution is scarce.

When AI's execution capability leaps from "barely usable" to "exceeding the professional level of the vast majority of humans," this premise becomes invalid.

What becomes invalid is not just the value of a certain type of work, but the entire organizational logic built on the foundation of "execution scarcity."

What is the new scarcity?

**Intent**. Knowing what you want, knowing what the goal is, knowing what "doing it right" means—this is the most scarce capability right now.

**Judgment**. When AI provides multiple options, knowing which one to choose, and knowing why to choose it.

**Interpersonal**. Understanding what another person truly needs, building trust, maintaining relationships.

**Creativity**. Generating combinations that even AI has never seen.

These four scarcities happen to fall exactly outside the scope that traditional enterprise organizational structures are best at managing. What traditional enterprises excel at managing is: task allocation, execution supervision, progress tracking, quality control—and these happen to be the things that AI can now do very well.

This is a fundamental misalignment.

## 1.3 The Structural Arrogance of Traditional Enterprises

The arrogance of traditional enterprises comes from their moats: scale, capital, licenses, brands, channels, and talent accumulation. These moats have been truly effective over the past fifty years.

But the challenges these moats face in the AI era are:

**Scale**: When a 5-person TSC can use AI to do what a 500-person traditional company does, scale transforms from a moat into a sunk cost.

**Capital**: When computing power costs drop exponentially, when open-source models can be used for free, and when cloud services drive the marginal cost of infrastructure close to zero, capital barriers have already been significantly lowered in many fields.

**Licenses**: This is one of the few truly effective barriers, but licenses protect access, not capability. A traditional enterprise with a license but poor execution capability can acquire capability by cooperating with a TSC, while the TSC can gain access through cooperation. This is exactly the foundation of the "TSC commanding traditional enterprises" model.

**Brand**: The essence of a brand is trust. When users can find products that truly meet their needs more precisely with the help of AI, the role of a brand shifts from "I know this name so I trust it" to "this product really suits me." This is advantageous for TSCs that can iterate quickly and deeply understand users.

**Channels**: Traditional channels are being eroded by the intent layer. When users make purchasing decisions through AI assistants, traditional shelves, shopping guides, and sales funnels are all bypassed.

**Talent accumulation**: This is the most complex one. The good news is that talent always has value. The bad news is that traditional enterprises have accumulated "execution-oriented talent," whereas the AI era requires "orchestration-oriented talent." The cultivation paths for these two types of talent are completely different.

Much of the arrogance of traditional enterprises comes from a misjudgment of the speed of this shift. They see AI demonstrations, find them interesting, but don't believe they can truly be implemented in their industries. This misjudgment gives TSCs a time window.

## 1.4 The Story of a Quality Inspector and a Blogger

Let us use two real individuals to illustrate this era's fault line.

**Person A**: A manufacturing quality inspector, earning 6,000 yuan a month, worked for eight years, highly skilled. Last year, the factory introduced visual inspection AI, and he was reassigned to "supervise the AI," with his salary dropping to 4,800. This year, he is delivering takeout, because the factory no longer even needs the "supervise the AI" position.

He is not lazy, not stupid, not negligent. He lost out because the market value of the skill he possessed (quality inspection) evaporated—not depreciated, but evaporated. In this process, there was no warning, no transition period, no retraining.

**Person B**: A short-video blogger with hundreds of thousands of followers. Last year he started using AI to create content; what used to require three people he now does alone, and does it better than before. Now he is thinking about how to "manage" his several AIs—because they have started to plan tasks on their own.

The word he uses, "manage," might soon seem naive, just like someone saying "I am managing my folders" twenty years ago. But he is moving upward, and the speed is accelerating.

The distance between these two people is widening at a speed that leaves no time to react.

Thin-Shell Company 2.0, in a sense, is to enable more people to walk Person B's path, rather than unknowingly walking Person A's path.

# Chapter 2: Critique of Pioneers—Historical Thin-Shell Attempts

## 2.1 Hollow Corporation 1.0: The Right Direction, the Wrong Tools

The concept of the "Hollow Corporation" first appeared in *Business Week* in 1986. The definition at the time was: an enterprise retains core design and brand, outsources manufacturing, logistics, and even most operations to third parties, keeping only a "hollow" coordination layer for itself.

This idea flourished in the 1990s, forming the "virtual organization" theory. Davidow and Malone, in their 1992 book *The Virtual Corporation*, provided the classic definition:

> "A temporary network of independent companies linked by IT, sharing technology, costs, and access to each other's markets"

Nike is the iconic case of this model. Nike essentially does not manufacture shoes; it designs, markets, and distributes—manufacturing is entirely outsourced to Asian factories. Apple is also this model to some extent, with its core being design and the ecosystem, and manufacturing being Foxconn.

This model is correct. It proved in the 1990s: **owning capability is more important than owning assets**.

But Hollow Company 1.0 has several fatal limitations that could not be overcome before the emergence of AI:

**Limitation 1: Execution relies on humans**. Outsourcing still means outsourcing to people, and people have their limitations: cost, quality fluctuations, communication costs, and trust-building costs. When a hollow company coordinates 50 outsourced vendors, it requires a sizable internal coordination team. This coordination team is itself a cost center, and as it scales, it becomes a bureaucracy.

**Limitation 2: Information asymmetry cannot be eliminated**. Information known to the outsourced party is unknown to the core layer of the hollow company, and vice versa. This information asymmetry leads to efficiency losses in collaboration, spawning numerous middle management layers, supervisory layers, and audit mechanisms.

**Limitation 3: High costs of formation and dissolution**. Forming temporary networks requires contract negotiations, due diligence, and a running-in period, with time costs often amounting to several months. Upon dissolution, there are contract termination fees, knowledge retention issues, and personnel resettlement issues. This renders "form at any time, dissolve at any time" an ideal rather than a reality.

**Limitation 4: Lack of a social layer**. The hollow company is a purely commercial collaboration, lacking the social cohesion to sustain long-term cooperation. When commercial interests conflict, the relationship immediately disintegrates.

The emergence of AI has dissolved a significant portion of Limitations 1, 2, and 3. This gives the correct direction of Hollow Company 1.0 the opportunity to be re-realized with the right tools.

## 2.2 Virtual Organization Theory: Genuine Insights from the Academic Ivory Tower

From the 1990s to the 2000s, academia conducted extensive research on virtual organizations, yielding some genuinely valuable insights. However, these insights long remained confined to papers and could not be implemented in reality.

**Insight 1: Loose coupling is a superpower**. In 1976, Weick proposed the concept of "loosely coupled systems," arguing that the various parts of an organization can respond to one another while maintaining their independence, making the organization both flexible and stable. Loose coupling is more adaptable to complex, rapidly changing environments than tight coupling.

This insight is extremely accurate when applied to the TSC: the various components of a TSC (human orchestrators, various Agents, payment layer, community) are loosely coupled—they collaborate, but do not depend on each other, and replacing any single part will not cause the entire system to crash.

**Insight 2: Boundary permeability**. A defining feature of virtual organizations is "permeable boundaries"—participants can simultaneously belong to multiple organizations, and membership is fluid rather than fixed. This is impermissible in traditional companies (employees cannot simultaneously work for competitors), but it is the norm in the context of virtual organizations.

TSC 2.0 takes this characteristic to the extreme: a single human orchestrator can participate in multiple TSCs simultaneously; a single Agent can be invoked by multiple TSCs; a single piece of knowledge can flow across multiple TSCs.

**Insight 3: Trust replaces control**. In traditional organizations, coordination is achieved through hierarchy and rules (bureaucracy); in virtual organizations, coordination is achieved through trust. However, academia never solved the problem of "how to quickly establish trust between strangers."

TSC 2.0 solves this problem with two mechanisms: large models provide transparent, verifiable execution records (you can see what the AI did and why it did it), and on-chain payments provide immutable asset transfer records. These two mechanisms enable trust to be quickly established between people who do not fully know each other.

Before the emergence of AI and blockchain, these academic insights could only remain theoretical. Now, technological maturity gives them the opportunity to be implemented in reality.

## 2.3 DAO: Experiments and Limitations of On-Chain Governance

The Decentralized Autonomous Organization (DAO) is the closest spiritual ancestor to Thin-Shell Company 2.0, and its most valuable frame of reference.

The core innovation of the DAO is: replacing rules and management with code (smart contracts), replacing equity with tokens, and replacing board decisions with blockchain voting. In theory, it achieves:

- Transparency: All decisions and financial flows are auditable on-chain
- Trustless collaboration: No need to know each other, only need to trust the code
- Borderless: Anyone with a wallet can participate
- Automated execution: Rules are enforced by code, requiring no human intervention

These characteristics highly overlap with those of TSC 2.0. However, DAOs have exposed several core problems in practice:

**Problem 1: Extremely low governance efficiency**. DAO decision-making is implemented through proposals and voting. However, the reality is that most token holders do not vote, causing decision-making power to actually concentrate in the hands of a few active participants. This neither achieves decentralization nor retains efficiency.

**Problem 2: Alienation of the token mechanism**. Tokens were originally governance tools, but quickly became speculative instruments. A large number of holders buy tokens for price appreciation, not to participate in governance. This renders the DAO's "community" existent in name only.

**Problem 3: Excessively high technical barriers**. Creating wallets, managing private keys, understanding smart contracts—this entire workflow is an insurmountable obstacle for 99% of non-technical people. "Join at any time" is a slogan in the DAO, but a lie in practice.

**Problem Four: Unable to Interface with the Real World**. DAOs run on-chain, but the tasks they need to accomplish are often off-chain. If a DAO wants to hire a designer to complete a design, the contract, delivery confirmation, and quality assessment in between—all of these must return to the real world, and DAO mechanisms are powerless regarding them.

**Problem Five: Lack of an Execution Layer**. A DAO is merely a governance mechanism, not an execution mechanism. It decides "what to do," but "how to do it" still relies on humans, and humans are the most unreliable execution layer.

TSC 2.0 inherits the spirit of DAOs' transparency, borderless participation, and on-chain settlement, but replaces token voting with large models as the coordination mechanism, replaces complex on-chain operations with an intent layer, and replaces cold wallet addresses with communities.

Simply put: DAOs are blockchain-native democracies, while TSC 2.0 is an AI-native efficiency system.

However, these companies have no substantive innovation in organizational form—they are **product companies**, merely using AI tools to enable small teams to produce higher output. They lack:

- Intent interface (operating mode is still traditional software product interaction)

They are the most powerful proof of Thin-Shell Company 2.0 (small teams can do big things), but they are not the complete form of Thin-Shell Company 2.0.

# Chapter 3: Definition—What is Thin-Shell Company 2.0

## 3.1 Core Equation

**TSC 2.0 = Intent Layer × (Large Model Strategic Brain + Professional Agent Execution Swarm) × Human Orchestrator × Dual-Track Payment × Social Layer**

In this equation, every term is multiplicative, not additive. Without any single term, the system cannot be established:

- Without the **intent layer**: The system cannot start, and humans need to spend a lot of time describing requirements
- Without the **large model**: Execution collapses, returning to the inefficiency of human outsourcing
- Without the **professional Agent**: There is only generalization capability, unable to complete tasks requiring deep professional knowledge
- Without the **human orchestrator**: The system loses direction, and AI falls into meaningless loops
- Without the **dual-track payment**: Value creation cannot flow, and cooperation cannot be sustained
- Without the **social layer**: The system collapses at the first conflict of interest

Described in simpler terms:

> **Thin-Shell Company 2.0 is a new type of organization that takes intent as its starting point, AI swarms as its execution layer, human judgment as its guide, the community as its organizational interface, and dual-track settlement as its value exchange mechanism.**

The meaning of "thin-shell" is: its human layer is extremely thin, but the execution power wrapped within this thin shell is extremely strong—just like an egg, where the shell is only 0.3 mm thick, but inside is a complete living system.

## 3.2 Nine Key Attributes

**Attribute One: Flexible Architecture**

TSC 2.0 has no fixed organizational chart. Depending on different tasks, the combination of humans and Agents changes at any time. If market analysis is needed today, the orchestrator combines the research Agent, data analysis Agent, and competitor monitoring Agent; if content production is needed tomorrow, a different set is combined. The architecture is a function of the task, rather than the task submitting to the architecture.

**Attribute Two: Flexible Industry**

TSC 2.0 is not bound to any specific industry. The same set of core methodologies and toolchains can be used for AI consulting, content production, code development, logistics optimization, education, medical assistance, financial analysis... Industry flexibility means: when the opportunity window of one industry closes, TSC can quickly migrate to the next window without needing to rebuild the entire organization.

**Attribute Three: Composable (TSC can join TSC)**

This is one of the most unique attributes of TSC 2.0. A TSC can become a sub-unit of another, larger TSC, just like Lego bricks can be pieced together.

For example: a TSC doing AI content production (Content TSC) can temporarily combine with a TSC doing AI data analysis (Data TSC) to take on a project that requires both content and data capabilities. The two TSCs operate collaboratively on this project; after the project ends, both TSCs return to their independent states, and next time they can recombine with different TSCs.

This composability creates an organizational ecology similar to biological cells: a single cell can survive independently, multiple cells can form tissues, and tissues can form more complex systems.

**Attribute Four: Can Command Traditional Enterprises**

TSC 2.0 can cooperate with traditional enterprises in the role of the "brain"—TSC provides core algorithms, data processing, and strategic judgment, while traditional enterprises provide physical resources, licenses, and human execution.

This is not an equal partnership, but a complementary one of capabilities. TSC's scarcity lies in cognition and orchestration capabilities; traditional enterprises' scarcity lies in assets and access. The combination of both is more competitive than either acting alone.

**Attribute Five: Social-Business-Psychological Trinity**

TSC 2.0 is not a purely commercial organization. It operates simultaneously on three levels: the business level (value creation and exchange), the social level (trust and connection between people), and the psychological level (participants' emotional states, sense of identity, sense of meaning). These three levels influence each other: a healthy psychological level makes the social level deeper, and a deep social level makes the business level more stable.

**Attribute Six: Dual-Track Payment**

TSC 2.0 supports both traditional payments (WeChat, Alipay, bank transfers) and on-chain payments (stablecoins, smart contract revenue sharing) during the transition period. The two tracks are not a replacement relationship, but complementary: the traditional track handles local, highly compliance-required scenarios where counterparts are unfamiliar with the chain; the on-chain track handles cross-border, multi-party revenue sharing, and scenarios requiring automatic execution.

**Attribute Seven: Join Anytime**

The participation threshold for TSC 2.0 is close to zero. No formal contract is needed (or only an extremely simple confirmation of intent is required), no equity negotiation, no background check, and no need to pass an interview. A person brings their capabilities in, does a task, receives the settlement, and that constitutes complete participation.

The flip side of "join anytime" is "exit anytime." The price of a low barrier to entry is a low barrier to exit. But this is exactly healthy: those who do not contribute value naturally leave, and those who do contribute value naturally stay, without needing any coercive mechanism.

**Attribute Eight: Model Combination is Company Architecture**

The architecture of a traditional company is: CEO, CTO, CFO, directors of various departments, middle managers, and executive-level employees. Each position has fixed responsibilities, reporting relationships, and compensation systems.

The "architecture" of TSC 2.0 is: general large model (strategic coordination), professional models (domain execution), memory and knowledge base (continuous accumulation), and human orchestrator (intent input and judgment). Different tasks activate different "departments"—this "department" is not written on an organizational chart, but is temporarily orchestrated.

**Attribute Nine: Chat is Launch**

The operational interface of TSC 2.0 is conversation. You don't need to open specific software, fill out a form, or initiate a project management process. You say "Let's do X" in a WeChat group, the AI understands this intent, starts coordinating resources, tells you what inputs are needed, and automatically begins the parts that can be automated.

The technical prerequisite for "chat to launch" is the intent layer—the AI's ability to extract structured tasks from natural language and route the tasks to the correct execution layer. This technology is basically mature in 2025.

## 3.3 What It Is Not

Clarifying boundaries is just as important as clear definition.

**TSC 2.0 is not an outsourcing company**. The core of an outsourcing company is to contract tasks out, acting only as a Shell. The core of TSC 2.0 is using AI as the execution layer and humans as the judgment layer, which is completely different.

**TSC 2.0 is not a DAO**. The core of a DAO is on-chain governance; the core of TSC 2.0 is AI execution. TSC 2.0 uses on-chain payments but does not use on-chain voting or token governance.

**TSC 2.0 is not a freelancers' association**. A combination of freelancers is loose, temporary, and lacks accumulation. TSC 2.0 has a clear structure, a knowledge accumulation mechanism, a reputation system, and repeatable operational modes.

**TSC 2.0 is not a collection of AI tools**. Buying a bunch of AI tools and using Claude, Midjourney, and Notion every day is not TSC 2.0. TSC 2.0 integrates these tools into a complete system with an intent layer, a payment layer, and a social layer.

**TSC 2.0 is not another type of startup**. The goal of a traditional startup is financing → growth → IPO/acquisition. The goal of TSC 2.0 is: rapid value creation, rapid settlement, and continuous accumulation. It does not necessarily have to become a registered company, nor does it necessarily need financing.

| Cultural carrier | Office + rules and regulations | None | Discord + tokens | **Community + psychological contract + AI memory** |

# Chapter 4: Three Organizational Forms

## 4.1 Form A: Single-Kernel TSC (Guerrilla Model)

**Definition**: The minimum TSC unit consisting of 1 to 5 human orchestrators as the kernel, assembling AI Agent groups around a clear intent to accomplish specific tasks.

**Typical scale**: 1-5 people + 5-20 Agent configurations

**Typical operational cycle**: Days to months

**Suitable scenarios**:
- Independent developers taking on projects (AI consulting, content creation, code development)
- Content operations for personal brands (content production, data analysis, audience interaction all AI-driven)
- Small-scale research tasks (market research, competitive analysis, industry reports)
- One-off solution delivery

**Internal structure**:```
Intent initiator (Human orchestrator)
    ↓
General large model (Task decomposition + Coordination)
    ↓ ↓ ↓
[Research Agent] [Writing Agent] [Analysis Agent] ...
    ↓
Result output
    ↓
Settlement (Traditional/On-chain)
```**Core competitiveness**: Extremely low startup costs, extremely fast response speed, almost zero coordination friction.

**Real-world case reference**: An AI content creator uses Claude for article structure, Midjourney for illustrations, ElevenLabs for audio versions, and a custom Agent for publishing and data tracking, achieving the output and quality of a three-person team alone. This is a single-kernel TSC.

**Key mechanism — the complete process from intent to delivery**:

1. **Intent input**: Describe the task in natural language within WeChat/Telegram/any chat tool
2. **Task decomposition**: The general large model breaks down the intent into subtasks, evaluating the priority and dependency relationships of each subtask
3. **Resource matching**: Automatically select or suggest appropriate Agents based on subtask characteristics
4. **Parallel execution**: Each Agent processes subtasks that do not depend on each other in parallel, and processes dependent subtasks serially
5. **Quality check**: The large model checks the consistency and completeness of each Agent's output
6. **Human judgment point**: At key nodes, present options to the human orchestrator and wait for judgment
7. **Final delivery**: Integrate all outputs to form the final deliverable
8. **Settlement trigger**: Upon task completion, automatically trigger the settlement process (if it's an on-chain payment) or prompt for manual settlement

**The moat of a single-kernel TSC**:

A single-kernel TSC appears fragile—only one or two people, with no organizational depth. But its moat actually lies in two areas:

First, **the accumulation of the orchestrator's personal judgment**. The same orchestrator operating repeatedly in the same field accumulates not just skills, but judgment—knowing when AI is right and when it is wrong, knowing how to articulate intent to achieve the best results, knowing which task combinations have a chemical reaction. This kind of judgment cannot be easily replicated.

Second, **the depth of the knowledge base**. Over time, a single-kernel TSC accumulates a private knowledge base: past projects completed, lessons learned, specific client preferences, unique industry insights. This knowledge base is fed to the large model, making it more knowledgeable about a specific domain than a general large model. This is the true moat.

## 4.2 Form B: Nested TSC (Combined Fleet Model)

**Definition**: Multiple Form A single-kernel TSCs temporarily combine into a composite TSC to fulfill a larger intent, where one TSC plays the "orchestrator" role and the other TSCs play the "execution unit" roles.

**Typical scale**: 3-10 single-kernel TSCs, totaling 10-50 people + a large number of Agents

**Typical operational cycle**: Several weeks to several months

**Suitable scenarios**:
- Large project delivery (requires a combination of multiple professional capabilities)
- Industry solution development (requires multi-party collaboration across technology, industry knowledge, and business)
- Cross-national collaboration projects
- Temporary expansion of a Thin-Shell Company (receiving a project that exceeds single-kernel capacity)

**Internal structure**:```
【Main Orchestration TSC】 (Human + General large model)
    ↓ Assign tasks ↑ Integrate output
    ↓              ↑
【Execution TSC-A】  【Execution TSC-B】  【Execution TSC-C】
（Content expertise）    （Technical expertise）    （Data expertise）
Human+Agent group      Human+Agent group      Human+Agent group
```**Key Designs of the Nesting Mechanism**:

**Protocol Layer**: Collaboration between TSCs does not require lawyers, but it does require an explicit "collaboration agreement." This agreement can be a natural language description generated by the LLM of the primary orchestration TSC, with both parties expressing consent through a simple confirmation. The agreement includes: task description, deliverable definition, quality standards, timeline, settlement method and amount, and exit clauses.

**Interface Layer**: What each TSC exposes to other TSCs is not internal operational details, but a clear "capability interface"—"what I can do, what input format I accept, what output format I produce, how long it takes, and what the quote is." This makes collaboration between TSCs as concise as calling an API.

**Settlement Layer**: After the nested TSC completes the project, profit sharing follows the "contribution ratio" principle. The LLM suggests a profit-sharing ratio based on the task volume, difficulty, and actual completion quality of each executing TSC; the primary orchestration TSC makes the final decision, and the profit sharing is executed on-chain.

**Real-Case Simulation**:

Suppose a government agency commissions someone to produce a research report and action plan on "AI applied to traditional manufacturing transformation."

- The order is taken by a single-kernel TSC with a policy research background (primary orchestration TSC)
- It temporarily integrates a single-kernel TSC with rich experience in AI implementation for manufacturing (providing cases and technical details)
- It integrates a single-kernel TSC skilled in industrial data analysis (providing data support)
- It integrates a single-kernel TSC skilled in policy document writing (responsible for the format and language of the final report)

The four TSCs collaborate in a shared Telegram group, each using its own Agent system to handle its respective tasks, aggregating outputs via shared documents, and ultimately delivering a report better than any single TSC could produce alone.

Upon project completion, the four TSCs share profits as agreed and return to independence.

**Advantages and Risks of the Joint Fleet**:

Advantages:
- Rapidly acquire professional capabilities that a single-kernel TSC lacks
- Risk is dispersed among TSCs
- Each TSC is only responsible for the part it excels at, resulting in higher quality

Risks:
- Coordination costs increase with the number of TSCs (an eternal problem)
- Information security (the information each TSC sees may contain sensitive data)
- Quality standard alignment (different TSCs may have inconsistent views on quality)

**Risk Countermeasures**:
- Control the number of nesting layers (recommended not to exceed two layers of nesting)
- Define an "information isolation zone" (specifying which information only the primary orchestration TSC can view)
- Specify quality standards and dispute resolution mechanisms in the collaboration agreement

## 4.3 Form C: TSC Commands Traditional Enterprises (Brain-Hands Model)

This is the most disruptive organizational form of TSC 2.0, and the most direct implementation of the goal to "strike the arrogance of traditional enterprises."

**Definition**: The TSC acts in the role of a "cognition-intensive kernel," collaborating with traditional enterprises that possess physical resources or specific access qualifications. The TSC provides decision support, data analysis, process optimization, and innovation direction, while the traditional enterprise provides execution capability, physical assets, licenses, and channels.

**Power Relationship**: It is not an equal collaboration, but rather **complementary capabilities, each taking what it needs**. The TSC's bargaining power comes from its cognitive and data advantages; the traditional enterprise's bargaining power comes from its asset and access advantages. Whoever is more scarce holds more sway.

**Suitable Scenarios**:

Scenario 1: **AI Transformation of Traditional Manufacturing**  
TSC provides: Production data analysis, quality inspection algorithms, supply chain optimization models  
Traditional enterprise provides: Factories, equipment, workers, production licenses  
Collaboration model: TSC charges an "AI optimization service fee" (fixed fee + performance-based profit sharing)

Scenario 2: **AI Assistance for Traditional Medical Institutions**  
TSC provides: Diagnostic assistance algorithms, patient data analysis, process optimization recommendations  
Traditional hospital provides: Medical licenses, doctor resources, patient data  
Collaboration model: TSC charges a technical service fee and signs a data usage agreement

Scenario 3: **AI-driven Operations for Traditional Retailers**  
TSC provides: Demand forecasting models, personalized recommendation systems, inventory optimization solutions  
Traditional retailer provides: Store networks, supplier relationships, brand recognition  
Collaboration model: TSC shares profits based on optimization outcomes

Scenario 4: **Government Digital Transformation**  
TSC provides: Policy analysis models, public service AI assistants, data visualization  
Government agency provides: Data authorization, budget, official endorsement  
Collaboration model: Project-based contract + long-term technical advisory services

**Competitive Logic of the Brain-Hands Model**:

When traditional enterprises think about "the threat of AI to us," they wonder "whether AI will replace our employees." But the real direction of the threat is: **a TSC can become the brain of a traditional enterprise, making the traditional enterprise's execution capability serve the TSC's goals**.

Once a TSC acquires manufacturing data and trains a professional model for this manufacturing scenario, it masters the ability to make the traditional factory's machines operate more efficiently. At this point, it is the traditional enterprise that needs the TSC, not the other way around.

This is the true meaning of "command": not order and control, but **possessing the core value that the other party cannot replace you for**.

**Key Design Points of Collaboration Agreements**:

1. **Data Ownership Clause**: Clearly define the intellectual property ownership of models trained by the TSC using traditional enterprise data. Common recommendation: the model itself belongs to the TSC, but the deployment authorization for this specific enterprise is permanent (or long-term), and the TSC must not use its data to serve peer competitors.

2. **Performance Metric Definition**: How to measure the effects generated by the collaboration (e.g., X% increase in production efficiency, Y% decrease in error rate); this is the basis for settlement. Metrics must be clarified before collaboration to avoid post-hoc disputes.

3. **Exit Clause**: If the TSC wishes to exit the collaboration, which core functions of the traditional enterprise will be affected? How long will the transition take? This must be addressed in the agreement.

4. **Non-Compete Clause (Variant)**: A TSC serving multiple competitors in the same industry needs to be addressed in terms of business ethics, but it may be difficult to constrain legally. It is recommended to solve this problem through information isolation (strict separation of data and models for different clients) rather than signing non-compete agreements.

## 4.4 Transformation Mechanisms Between Forms

An important characteristic of TSC 2.0 is that the three forms can transform into one another, and the transformation cost is extremely low.

**A → B (Single-kernel expands to nested)**:
When a single-kernel TSC takes on a project beyond its capacity, it can temporarily connect to other single-kernel TSCs to form a consortium. This transformation does not require any organizational change—it merely adds external TSCs as partners during the project execution period. Once the project ends, it returns to the single-kernel state.

**B → A (Nested contracts to single-kernel)**:
After the joint fleet completes its mission, each TSC resumes independent operation. There is no dismissal, no offboarding process, and no knowledge loss (each TSC retains the experience accumulated during the collaboration).

**A/B → C (Extending towards traditional enterprise)**:
When a single-kernel or nested TSC accumulates sufficiently deep professional knowledge and data advantages in a certain industry, it can proactively propose a "brain-hands" collaboration to a traditional enterprise. This is a proactive strategic transformation, not a reactive response.

**C → A/B (Independence from traditional enterprise)**:
If the collaboration between the TSC and the traditional enterprise generates sufficient knowledge accumulation, the TSC can generalize this knowledge to serve more clients, no longer relying on a single traditional enterprise partner. This is the critical path for a TSC to achieve true independence and avoid being hijacked by any single traditional enterprise.

# Chapter 5: Social Layer—The Infrastructure Neglected by Everyone

## 5.1 Why Commercial Organizations Need a Social Layer

This is the part of Thin-Shell Company 2.0 that is most easily misunderstood and most easily neglected.

When most people talk about "new company forms in the AI era," they talk about tools, efficiency, and business models. The social layer—relationships between people, emotions, psychological contracts—is almost never on the agenda of business discussions.

This is a serious mistake.

Let's look at a simple fact: the vast majority of startups do not die from technical problems, nor do they die from market problems, but from **human problems**. Breakdowns in founder relationships, team infighting, loss of direction, loss of a sense of meaning. These are all problems of the social layer.

For TSC 2.0, this issue is more pressing, rather than easier. Traditional companies have many mechanisms to maintain the functions of the social layer: physical space (working together), formal structure (positions, reporting relationships), economic binding (salary, equity). Although these mechanisms are imperfect, they are effective.

TSC 2.0 deliberately abandons most of these mechanisms—no fixed office, no strict hierarchy, and economic relationships are loose project-based systems. This makes TSC 2.0 structurally more flexible, but also makes its social stickiness naturally weaker.

This is why the social layer must be designed, rather than allowed to grow naturally.

## 5.2 Psychological Contract: TSC's Implicit Constitution

The psychological contract is a concept from organizational psychology, referring to the **unwritten mutual expectations** between an organization and its members.

In traditional companies, the psychological contract usually includes: the company expects employees to arrive on time, complete tasks earnestly, and be loyal to the company; employees expect the company to provide stable compensation, career development opportunities, and fair treatment. These expectations are not written in the contract, but their violation (by either party) will directly affect employees' work motivation and organizational stickiness.

TSC 2.0 needs an explicit psychological contract design, because it lacks the guarantee of traditional contracts and the natural stickiness of physical co-presence, relying entirely on the psychological contract to maintain the stability of collaboration.

**TSC 2.0's psychological contract should include the following**:

**Commitments to participants**:
- Your contributions will be seen and fairly settled
- Your opinions will be heard, even if the final decision does not follow them
- You can exit without penalty
- The abilities and reputation you accumulate here belong to you, not to anyone else

**Expectations of participants**:
- You come here bringing real abilities and contributions, not identities and positions
- You are responsible for your own tasks and do not need to be pushed
- You do not engage in zero-sum games here; you do not steal others' credit or push away your own responsibility
- When you encounter problems, you speak directly, not gossiping behind the scenes

**Core principles**:
- Transparency: You know what TSC is doing, and you know how your contributions are calculated
- Fairness: The settlement mechanism is public, contestable, but ultimately fair
- Respect: Your time and abilities are respected; there are no meaningless reports and meetings

These contents can be declared in simple language in a chat group when the TSC is established, drafted with the help of large models. No legal documents are needed, but all core participants must explicitly express their agreement.

**Applicable scenarios for different types of psychological contracts**:

| Contract type | Participant relationship | Suitable scenario | Stickiness strength |
|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Mission contract** | "We are doing something meaningful together" | Long-term collaboration, value-driven projects | Strongest |
| **Ability contract** | "I become stronger here" | Technology growth-oriented TSC | Strong |
| **Commercial contract** | "I earn the money I deserve here" | Short-term projects, profit-oriented | Medium |
| **Learning contract** | "I encounter new knowledge and networks here" | Early participants, novices | Medium-weak |

A healthy TSC usually contains multiple contract types simultaneously: core members have mission contracts, executing members have ability or commercial contracts, and peripheral collaborators have commercial or learning contracts.

## 5.3 User Relationship Moat

In the AI era, the hardest asset to replicate is not algorithms (algorithms will be open-sourced, imitated, and surpassed), but **deep trust relationships with specific groups**.

This means for TSC 2.0: your competitiveness comes not only from the AI tools you use, but from the group of people you serve being willing to continue letting you serve them, willing to recommend you to others, and willing to support you when you need it.

**Three levels of user relationships**:

**First level: Transactional relationship**  
A client seeks you to solve a problem, you solve it, charge a fee, and it ends. This is the most basic relationship, which can be maintained by product quality and price, but has no stickiness; the client can switch to the next cheaper, faster service provider at any time.

**Second level: Advisory relationship**  
The client treats you as a trusted advisor in a specific field. They don't just seek you to solve specific problems, but proactively consult you when facing decisions related to that field. This relationship is built on: your understanding of the client's history (memory), the accuracy of your predictions (trust), and your genuine concern for the client's interests (not just selling services).

**Third level: Ecological relationship**  
The client becomes a part of you, and you become a part of the client's ecosystem. Clients bring other clients to you, clients participate in your content production, clients share their experiences in your community, and clients become your resource nodes when you need them. This relationship is the true moat, almost impossible for competitors to directly replicate.

TSC 2.0 should proactively design: how to move client relationships from the first level to the third level?

Critical path:
- **Memory mechanism**: Use the long-term memory function of large models to accumulate each client's preferences, history, and context. Let every interaction continue based on the last one, rather than starting from scratch.
- **Transparency mechanism**: Let clients know what you are doing, why you are doing it, and what your judgments are based on. Transparency itself is one of the most effective ways to build trust.
- **Value feedback**: Proactively send clients information they have not requested but might find useful. Provide genuinely useful value even when clients have absolutely no intention to purchase.

## 5.5 Emotional Structure Design

The operation of TSC 2.0 is highly transparent, rapidly changing, and results-oriented. This places specific requirements and challenges on the emotional state of participants.

**Common Emotional Crises**:

**Crisis 1: Loss of Meaning**  
When AI can do more and more things, human participants may begin to doubt whether their contributions are truly valuable. "I'm just clicking the confirm button, what's the point?"

**Response Design**: Clarify that the human role is not an "executor," but an "intent owner" and "judge." Emphasize: AI knows how to do it, but doesn't know why to do it, nor what constitutes "doing it well"—these two things are the exclusive domains of humans.

**Crisis 2: Sense of Loss of Control**  
When many things are automatically completed by AI, human participants may feel they have lost control—"I'm not entirely clear on how this thing was accomplished."

**Response Design**: Transparency mechanism. Every important decision of the AI must have a traceable log, so humans can see at any time "what the AI is doing and why it is doing it this way." The sense of control comes from transparency, not necessarily from direct operation.

**Crisis 3: Sense of Loneliness**  
Without colleagues, without an office, primarily interacting with AI, some people will feel lonely and lose their sense of belonging.

**Response Design**: The community is not just a workplace, but also a social space. Regular non-work interactions (online and offline), celebrating milestones, sharing fragments of personal life—these are not wasting time, but the necessary cost of maintaining the operation of the social layer.

**Crisis 4: Performance Anxiety**  
TSC is results-oriented, lacking the "just showing up to work guarantees a salary" sense of security of traditional companies. This places immense psychological pressure on some people.

**Response Design**: Build a "sense of accumulation"—let participants see that even if a certain project does not have high immediate returns, their accumulation in the knowledge base, reputation, and skills is real. This long-term accumulation is the source of their sense of security.

## 5.6 Application and Transformation of the Dark Forest Law in TSC

You mentioned "the committee operates under the Dark Forest Law." This is a topic worth expanding upon seriously.

In Liu Cixin's *The Three-Body Problem*, the Dark Forest Law describes an extremely competitive cosmic survival logic: exposing oneself means being destroyed, so the most rational choice is silence and preemptive strike.

In the practical application of the business world, this logic transforms into: do not proactively expose your core capabilities and resources, because this information may be utilized by competitors. In certain highly competitive, high-interest domains, this logic is indeed effective.

But TSC 2.0, in most cases, requires a **transformed version of the Dark Forest Law**:

**Inside TSC: Garden Law**  
Inside TSC, there should be extreme transparency and extreme openness. There is no information asymmetry among core members, no strategic reservations, and no cliques. This internal transparency is the foundation for the healthy operation of the social layer.

**TSC External: Stratified Information Strategy**  
- To the public: Display results, display capabilities, build trust (proactive exposure, encouraging them to seek you out)
- To collaborating TSCs: Share project-related information, retain the core knowledge base and future plans (selective exposure)
- To competitors: Do not proactively expose core methodologies, but there is no need to deliberately hide them (passive protection)
- To traditional enterprises (the objects to be directed): Display value, establish irreplaceability (strategic exposure)

**Scenarios that truly require Dark Forest logic**:
- New tools/methods being developed, not exposed before they are ready
- Key customer relationships, not broadly promoted before the relationship is solidified
- Collaborations being negotiated, kept silent before they are finalized
- The structure and depth of the core knowledge base, this is the asset that least should be exposed

**Scenarios that do not require Dark Forest logic**:
- What tools you use (tools are public, the way they are combined is the secret)
- Your methodological framework (methodology can be public, execution is the barrier)
- Your values and principles of doing things (this is the most effective way to build trust)
- Your experiences of failure (this is the fastest path to building authenticity and trust)

# Chapter 6: AI Execution Layer—Who is Really Doing the Work

## 6.1 Large Models as "Strategic Brains"

The large model of TSC 2.0 is not an "assistant," it is a "strategic partner." This distinction is crucial.

An assistant is: you tell it what to do, and it does it.  
A strategic partner is: you tell it what result you want, it tells you what you should do, and begins coordinating execution.

The most powerful large models currently (2026) have already approached or exceeded human levels in the following aspects:

- **Task decomposition**: Breaking down a complex intent into a sequence of interdependent subtasks
- **Resource matching**: Determining which tool or type of Agent should execute each subtask
- **Quality assessment**: Making critical evaluations of the outputs of other models or humans
- **Anomaly detection**: Identifying deviations and risks during the execution process
- **Knowledge synthesis**: Integrating information from different sources into a consistent conclusion

But large models still have obvious limitations:

- **Will**: It lacks sustained intent; context must be re-established with every conversation (this is the problem memory systems aim to solve)
- **Boundaries of judgment**: In highly ambiguous, highly personalized judgments, it may offer advice that appears reasonable but is actually unsuitable for your specific context
- **Hallucinations**: It outputs erroneous information with high confidence, which poses a real danger in high-risk domains such as healthcare and law
- **Depth of emotional understanding**: It can comprehend emotions but cannot truly feel them, which becomes a bottleneck in interpersonal scenarios requiring deep empathy

These limitations precisely define the human role in TSC: handling the parts that require genuine emotion, sustained will, and true intuition.

**Selection principles for large models**:

TSC 2.0 should select appropriate large models based on task characteristics:

| Task Type | Recommended Model Characteristics | Typical Representatives |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Strategic planning, complex reasoning | Strongest reasoning, high context window | Claude Opus, GPT-4o |
| Code generation and debugging | Code-specific enhancement | Claude Code, Cursor |
| Chinese content creation | Chinese comprehension and generation | Claude series, domestic open-source models |
| Fast, lightweight tasks | High speed, low cost | Claude Haiku, GPT-4o-mini |
| Multimodal tasks | Image + text comprehension | GPT-4o, Gemini |

**Do not rely on just one model**. Different models possess different strengths; combining them is more efficient and safer than single-model dependency.

## 6.2 Specialized Models as "Skilled Workers"

Beyond general-purpose large models, the execution layer of TSC 2.0 also requires numerous specialized models. Fine-tuned for specific tasks, these models are more accurate, more efficient, and lower-cost than general models.

**Classification of specialized models**:

**Domain-specific models**:
- Medical diagnosis assistance models (significantly higher accuracy than general models in medical scenarios)
- Legal document analysis models (understand the specific logic of legal language)
- Financial data analysis models (understand financial statements and market data)
- Code review specialized models (focus on code security and quality)

**Capability-specific models**:
- Image generation models (Midjourney, FLUX, SD)
- Voice synthesis models (ElevenLabs, TTS)
- Video generation models (Sora, Kling)
- Data analysis models (specifically process structured data)

**Tool invocation models**:
- Web browsing and information scraping
- Code execution and testing
- File processing (PDF, Excel, Word)
- Database querying

**Usage strategies for specialized models**:

TSC 2.0 does not need to train its own specialized models (unless it has accumulated sufficiently unique data in a specific industry). The strategy should be:

1. **Understand the available specialized model ecosystem**: Keep an eye on new developments from Hugging Face and various specialized API providers
2. **Evaluate and test**: For each common task type, test the performance of multiple specialized models
3. **Build a "tool library"**: Compile a list of specialized models commonly used by your TSC, including cost, accuracy, and usage limitations
4. **Dynamic updates**: AI tools iterate extremely fast; reassess whether the tool library needs updating every quarter

## 6.3 General Models as "Full-Stack Execution"

Beyond large models and specialized models, TSC 2.0 also requires a layer of "general execution capability"—the ability to complete specific operational tasks based on instructions from large models.

This layer corresponds to **Agent frameworks and automation tools**:

- **Task orchestration frameworks**: LangChain, AutoGen, CrewAI—organizing multi-model invocations into executable workflows
- **RPA tools (Robotic Process Automation)**: Automatically completing repetitive UI operations (clicking, form filling, data entry)
- **Data pipelines**: Automatically scraping, cleaning, and transforming data
- **Scheduled tasks**: Automatically executing specific tasks at set times (data updates, report generation, monitoring alerts)

**Key capability of general models: Persistent operation**

Unlike single-invocation large models, the general execution layer of TSC 2.0 needs to be capable of **persistent operation**:

- 24-hour monitoring of specific metrics (competitor pricing, social media mentions, market data)
- Automatically completing periodic tasks according to schedule
- Automatically triggering the next task upon completion of the previous one
- Proactively alerting the human orchestrator when anomalies are detected

This is the "silent production" mentioned in Chapter 1 of the whitepaper—while you sleep, TSC is working.

## 6.4 The Irreplaceable Role of Humans

In a system where AI's execution capability grows ever stronger, human value is not diminished, but rather **concentrated**—concentrated precisely where AI truly falls short.

**The four core roles of humans in TSC**:

**Role One: Intent Holder**  
You know what you want, know what "good" looks like, and know why this thing is worth doing. These are things AI cannot hold independently; they must be provided by humans and maintained throughout the execution process.

**Role Two: Value Judge**  
When AI provides options A, B, and C, humans judge which one better fits the actual situation, which is more appropriate in current interpersonal relationships, and which is wiser in the long run. This kind of judgment relies on experience, intuition, and a deep understanding of specific contexts—things AI cannot fully replace.

**Role Three: Relationship Maintainer**  
Client relationships, collaborative TSC relationships, community relationships—these require genuine human emotional investment to maintain. AI can help you draft communications, but real trust is built by humans.

**Role Four: Ethics Gatekeeper**  
AI executes according to instructions. When instructions produce unreasonable or harmful results, it is humans who identify and correct the problem. "This should not be done"—this judgment is ultimately human responsibility.

**Optimal Density of Human Participation**:

The operation of TSC 2.0 is neither "staring at AI 24 hours a day" nor "completely letting AI run on its own," but rather investing human attention at the right nodes.

Nodes requiring high human attention:
- Intent confirmation at project initiation
- Key decision nodes (usually directional, irreversible decisions)
- Client communication and relationship maintenance
- Final quality judgment of AI outputs

Nodes where human attention can be reduced:
- Data collection and organization
- Format conversion and document processing
- Draft generation (only requiring final human review)
- Scheduled reporting and monitoring

## 6.5 Design Principles of Model Combination

**Principle One: Large Models Orchestrate, Professional Models Execute**  
Do not use the most expensive large model for everything. Large models are responsible for understanding intent, coordinating execution, and evaluating results; specific execution tasks are handed over to appropriate professional models or tools.

**Principle Two: Redundancy is Not Waste**  
For critical outputs, use two different models to generate them separately, then compare the differences and synthesize the best. The probability of both models making an error simultaneously is far lower than that of a single model making an error.

**Principle Three: Human in the Loop**  
Do not completely remove human supervision nodes, especially those involving external delivery, client communication, and capital flow. The higher the degree of automation, the more important human confirmation at key nodes becomes.

**Principle Four: Cost Awareness**  
Large model API calls incur costs. When designing workflows, use cheap small models for simple tasks, and expensive large models only for tasks that truly require strong reasoning capabilities. As usage grows, this difference will become significant.

**Principle Five: Fail Gracefully**  
When designing a system, assume any component might fail. When an Agent fails, the system should be able to detect the failure, notify humans, and have an alternative path, rather than the entire workflow crashing.

## 6.6 Orchestrator: The Scarcest Capability in TSC

In a world where AI execution capability grows ever stronger, the scarcest resource is not executors, but **orchestrators**—those who know how to combine large models, professional models, toolchains, and human collaborators to get the whole thing done with high quality.

The core capability of an orchestrator is not technology, but **systems thinking**:

- Ability to decompose vague intents into executable task sequences
- Ability to identify which tasks have dependencies and which can be parallelized
- Ability to judge what tool or model should be used for each subtask
- Ability to evaluate the quality of AI outputs, knowing when results are good and when they need to be redone
- Ability to quickly diagnose causes and find repair paths when the system errors

Cultivating an excellent orchestrator requires three to six months of accumulation of practical experience, not three to six years. But these three to six months require real project experience, not just watching tutorials.

This capability is the most important source of competitive advantage for humans in TSC 2.0.

# Chapter 7: Intent Layer—Chat as Initiation

## 7.1 Definition and Evolution of the Intent Layer

The **Intent Layer** is an emerging technological and interaction paradigm that answers a fundamental question: **How can humans communicate their needs to AI systems with minimal friction?**

The human-computer interaction model of the internet era is:
- Human opens software
- Human finds features
- Human fills out forms according to the software's logic
- Human submits and waits for results

This model started with command-line interfaces in the 1970s, evolved to graphical interfaces, and then to mobile apps; the form has changed, but the essence remains the same: **humans adapt to the software's logic**.

The Intent Layer flips this relationship: **software adapts to human expression**. You say what you want, the system understands and executes, and you don't need to know what happens behind the scenes.

Three core capabilities of the Intent Layer:
1. **Natural Language Understanding**: Extracting structured intent from human language
2. **Task Decomposition**: Transforming intent into executable subtasks
3. **Tool Routing**: Routing subtasks to the correct tool or Agent

These three capabilities have reached practical levels in large models by 2025-2026.

## 7.2 From the APP Era to the Intent Era

Characteristics of the APP era (2008 to present):
- Each requirement corresponds to an APP
- Each APP has its own interaction logic
- People need to learn how to use each APP
- Data is isolated across different APPs

This model has produced the "APP fragmentation" problem—an ordinary person has dozens of APPs on their phone, each with its own database, unable to communicate with one another. The frequent locations you save in Amap are unknown to Meituan; the address you receive in WeChat is unknown to DingTalk.

Characteristics of the Intent era:
- Each requirement is described in natural language
- AI understands the intent and automatically invokes the correct combination of tools
- Tools are coordinated through AI, allowing data to flow across tools
- People only need to speak; they do not need to know how to use each tool

This is not a distant future. WeChat's AI features, upgraded versions of Siri, and various large language model-based assistant products are all early implementations of the intent layer.

The emergence of OpenClaw is a signal of the intent layer's large-scale implementation: when you can use WhatsApp (the world's most ubiquitous messaging APP) to control the AI on your computer to execute operations, the threshold of the intent layer drops from "exclusive to the tech-savvy" to "accessible to anyone".

## 7.3 WeChat/Telegram as an Operating System

For TSC 2.0, WeChat groups and Telegram channels are not merely communication tools, but the **operating system**—the interface and carrier for the entire TSC's operation.

Why chat tools, rather than dedicated project management software?

**Reason One: Zero Learning Cost**. Everyone knows how to use WeChat. No training required, no setup needed.

**Reason Two: Ubiquity**. WeChat is China's most ubiquitous communication tool; users do not need to open specific software and can participate in the TSC's operation in any context.

**Reason Three: Natural Recording**. Chat records themselves are decision records, execution records, and knowledge accumulation. There is no need for a separate document to record "what happened"; the group chat is the best log.

**Reason Four: AI Can Natively Exist Within**. Through API integration, an AI Bot can exist directly as a member in a WeChat group, receiving instructions, reporting results, and participating in discussions.

**Ideal TSC WeChat Group Structure**:```
[Project Main Group]
├── [Daily Collaboration Subgroup] (AI Bot resident, executing daily tasks)
├── [Decision Record Subgroup] (Only records important decisions, no daily communication)
└── [Client Communication Group] (Independent communication space with specific clients)
```**Roles of the AI Bot in the group**:
- Listen: Continuously monitor conversations in the group, identifying task commands and questions
- Execute: Automatically execute tasks that can be automated
- Report: Report task progress and results in the group
- Remind: Proactively @mention relevant people when human judgment is required
- Record: Organize decision logs, generate daily/weekly reports

## 7.4 The Complete Chain of Intent → Routing → Execution → Feedback

**Step 1: Intent Capture**

Users express their intent in the chat interface (which can be complete natural language or brief commands). The AI understands the intent; if the intent is not clear enough, it proactively asks for key information (without asking for information that does not affect execution).

The quality of intent capture directly determines the quality of subsequent execution. Good intent capture should confirm:
- What is the **goal** of the task (what result is desired)
- What is the **scope** of the task (to what extent it should be done)
- What are the **constraints** of the task (what cannot be done, what must be adhered to)
- What are the **time** requirements of the task (when it is needed)

**Step 2: Task Decomposition**

The general large model decomposes the intent into a specific sequence of subtasks, and:
- Identifies dependencies between subtasks (B can only start after A is completed)
- Marks which subtasks can be processed in parallel
- Marks the recommended execution tool for each subtask
- Provides an overall time estimate

The decomposition results are visible to the user, who can confirm or modify them.

**Step 3: Resource Routing**

Based on the task decomposition results, the system automatically:
- Calls the appropriate Agent or tool
- Passes the context required for each subtask
- Establishes data transfer pipelines between subtasks
- Sets monitoring points (triggering reminders when tasks timeout or fail)

**Step 4: Parallel Execution**

Each Agent and tool begins to execute subtasks that are not mutually dependent in parallel, and serially waits for dependent subtasks to complete. During execution, key progress nodes are reported to the user.

**Step 5: Quality Check**

The general large model performs a quality check on the output of each subtask:
- Is it complete (are there any missing key contents)
- Is it consistent (do the conclusions of each part contradict each other)
- Does it meet the requirements (whether format, style, and accuracy match the initial definition)

**Step 6: Human Judgment Node**

In the following situations, the system pauses automatic execution and waits for human judgment:
- Quality check identifies issues
- Decisions are found that only humans can make
- Subtask results need integration, but there are multiple feasible options for the integration method
- Final confirmation before external delivery or fund flow

**Step 7: Output and Feedback**

After the final deliverable is generated:
- Present the results to the user
- Record the key parameters and learned experiences of this task
- Add valuable content to the knowledge base
- If settlement trigger conditions exist, automatically initiate the settlement process

## 7.5 Insights from the OpenClaw Model for TSC

The most important insight from OpenClaw is not technical, but the **choice of access point**.

It did not create a new APP, nor a new platform, but chose to access WhatsApp—a tool used by billions of people worldwide every day.

This choice means: AI capabilities do not need to educate users to use new tools; they only need to let AI exist within the tools users are already accustomed to.

For TSC 2.0, this principle is: **Do not make collaborators change their work habits just to participate in TSC**. Let the operation of TSC be embedded in the tools they are already using.

In China, this means WeChat is the core access point. In international scenarios, it is WhatsApp, Telegram, Slack.

**Another insight from OpenClaw**:

Open source and ecosystem are super weapons. After OpenClaw was open-sourced, developers worldwide built thousands of Skills (functional plugins) on it. These Skills made OpenClaw's capability boundaries far broader than what any single company could develop alone.

The tool ecosystem of TSC 2.0 should follow the same logic: establish open interfaces, let community developers expand capabilities, rather than trying to develop all tools itself.

## 7.6 Design Specifications for the Intent Layer

If you are designing an intent layer for your TSC, here are the key design specifications:

**Specification 1: Accept incomplete input**  
Users do not need to express their intent very precisely. The system should be able to extract enough information from an "roughly like this" description to start working, and gradually clarify during the execution process.

**Specification 2: Follow-up questions must be precise**  
When follow-up questions are needed, only ask for information that is essential for execution but not provided by the user. Do not ask "who is your target audience" if you can infer it from the user's history.

**Specification 3: Interruptible and redirectable**  
Users can change their intent at any time during execution; the system should be able to smoothly redirect rather than start over.

**Specification 4: Results must be explainable**  
Every important decision made by the AI should be explainable in natural language as to "why it was done this way". Black-box decisions will make users lose their sense of control.

**Specification Five: Failures Must Be Explicit**  
When a task fails, the system should clearly tell the user: what failed, why, and what the user needs to do to recover. Do not silently skip failures, and do not describe errors in technical language.

# Chapter 8: Dual-Track Payments—Financial Architecture for the Transition Period

## 8.1 Why Dual-Track is Needed

"Why not just use on-chain payments" is a reasonable question. The answer is: the world will not switch overnight.

In mainland China, the following realities limit the feasibility of pure on-chain payments:

1. **Legal constraints**: China has strict controls on cryptocurrency transactions; in most scenarios, fiat currency remains the only legally recognized settlement method
2. **Counterparty readiness**: A large number of traditional enterprises and individuals do not have crypto wallets and cannot accept on-chain payments
3. **Exchange rate risk**: On-chain payments are typically denominated in USD stablecoins, involving legal and exchange rate risks related to RMB conversion
4. **Accounting compliance**: Corporate finance requires invoicing and accounting vouchers; pure on-chain income is difficult to process within the accounting systems of most enterprises

"Why not entirely use traditional payments" also has an answer:

1. **Cross-border inefficiency**: International wire transfers take 2-5 days, incur high fees, and are subject to various countries' financial regulatory restrictions
2. **Multi-party distribution difficulty**: When a project needs to distribute funds to participants in 10 different countries, the friction cost of the traditional payment system is extremely high
3. **Impossibility of automatic execution**: Traditional payments cannot be automatically triggered by smart contracts and require manual operation
4. **Stability**: For projects spanning longer cycles, on-chain stablecoins can circumvent some of the inflation risks of local currencies

The design of dual-track payments is precisely to retain the advantages of both payment capabilities during the transition period while circumventing their respective limitations.

## 8.2 Architecture Design of the Traditional Payment Track

**Mainland China Scenario**:```
Customer payment → Alipay/WeChat Pay/Bank Transfer
    ↓
TSC receiving account (Personal/Enterprise)
    ↓
Distribute to participants according to contribution
    ↓
Each participant receives payment via WeChat/Alipay
```**Notes**:
- Annual personal receipts exceeding a certain threshold may trigger tax reporting requirements
- If the TSC operation scale is large, it is recommended to register a sole proprietorship or company to standardize finances
- Invoice issues: If the client requires an invoice, a compliant invoicing entity is required

**International Client Scenarios**:```
Customer payment → Stripe/PayPal/SWIFT
    ↓
TSC receiving (Foreign currency account or Hong Kong account)
    ↓
Currency exchange
    ↓
Distribute to participants
```**Notes**:
- Stripe currently cannot be registered directly in mainland China, but can be accessed through a Hong Kong entity
- SWIFT wire transfers are suitable for large amounts; for small amounts, the transaction fee percentage is too high
- Foreign exchange earnings in China must be settled according to regulations; amounts exceeding $50,000 may require additional approval

## 8.3 Architecture Design of On-Chain Payment Rails

**Infrastructure Selection**:

It is recommended to use the combination of **Solana chain + USDC stablecoin**:

- Solana: Fast transaction speed (millisecond-level confirmation), extremely low transaction fees (less than $0.01 per transaction), mature ecosystem
- USDC: Pegged 1:1 to the US dollar, issued by Circle, compliant and transparent, good liquidity

**Multi-Party Split Smart Contract**:

Existing multi-party split protocols (such as Splits by 0xSplits) can be used, or a simple split contract can be customized:```
Project receiving address (Smart contract)
    ↓
Automatically distribute according to preset ratio
    ↓
Participant A wallet: 40%
Participant B wallet: 35%
Participant C wallet: 25%
```The revenue split ratio is set at the beginning of the project and automatically executed upon completion, requiring no manual operations and eliminating the need to trust any party.

**User Experience Optimization for On-Chain Payments**:

For participants unfamiliar with on-chain operations, custodial wallet services (such as Coinbase Custody or exchange custody) can be utilized. This eliminates the need for them to manage their own private keys, allowing them to directly convert on-chain receipts into fiat currency for withdrawal.

## 8.4 Dual-Track Synergy: Two Paths for the Same Money

The core design of dual-track payments is: **transparent to the customer, flexible in the backend**.

Customers do not need to know which track TSC uses to receive payments. The payment options TSC offers externally can be WeChat Pay, Alipay, bank transfers, Stripe, or even Bitcoin (for customers who accept cryptocurrency).

Within TSC, regardless of which track receives the funds, they all flow uniformly into TSC's financial management layer, and are then distributed according to participants' preferences: participants preferring traditional payments receive their share via WeChat or bank transfer; participants preferring on-chain settlement receive USDC directly to their wallets.

**Financial Architecture of Dual-Track Synergy**:```
[Revenue Layer]
Customer traditional payment ←→ TSC receiving center ←→ Customer on-chain payment

[Accounting Layer]
All income uniformly recorded (Currency conversion, exchange rate time point)

[Distribution Layer]
Calculate distribution amount according to contribution ratio

[Payment Layer]
↓ Traditional track                          ↓ On-chain track
WeChat/Alipay/Bank Transfer               USDC stablecoin/Solana
```## 8.5 Specific Case of Cross-Border Settlement and Multi-Party Split Accounting

**Case: Settlement of a Nested TSC Project Across Five Countries**

Project: Providing a Chinese market analysis report for a European client.

Participants:
- Main Orchestration TSC (Mainland China): Takes on the project, responsible for coordination, contribution ratio 40%
- Data Analysis TSC (Singapore): Provides data processing, contribution ratio 30%
- Report Writing TSC (Taiwan, China): Responsible for the final report, contribution ratio 20%
- Tool Provider (USA): Provides proprietary data access rights, contribution ratio 10%

Total project amount: 5000 USD

**If using traditional payment**:
- European client SWIFT to Mainland China Main Orchestration TSC: 3-5 days, transaction fee approx. $40
- Main Orchestration TSC SWIFT to Singapore TSC: 3-5 days, transaction fee approx. $30
- Main Orchestration TSC pays Taiwan TSC: 3-5 days, involves cross-border compliance
- Main Orchestration TSC pays US Tool Provider: PayPal or SWIFT, each has limitations

Total time: 2-3 weeks, total transaction fees: approx. $100+, involves multiple foreign exchange approvals

**If using on-chain payment**:
- European client sends 5000 USDC to the project receiving contract: < 5 seconds, transaction fee <$0.01
- Contract automatically allocates:
  - Main Orchestration TSC receives 2000 USDC (40%): < 5 seconds
  - Singapore TSC receives 1500 USDC (30%): simultaneously
  - Taiwan TSC receives 1000 USDC (20%): simultaneously
  - US Tool Provider receives 500 USDC (10%): simultaneously

Total time: < 1 minute, total transaction fees: <$0.05, no approvals required

This comparison serves as the most compelling justification for TSC 2.0's on-chain payment rail.

## 8.6 Transition Period Timeline

TSC 2.0's dual-track payment is a transitional design, not a permanent one. The following is an approximate evolutionary timeline (predicted based on current (2026) regulatory and technological trends):

**2026-2027 (Present - Near-term)**:
- Traditional payments dominate, on-chain payments used for specific cross-border scenarios
- Establish dual-track payment infrastructure
- Test and optimize on-chain split accounting mechanisms

**2028-2029 (Mid-term)**:
- As more countries/regions introduce stablecoin regulatory frameworks, the compliance of on-chain payments improves
- More clients begin to accept stablecoin payments
- The proportion of on-chain payments gradually rises to 30-50%

**2030 and beyond (Long-term)**:
- Most countries have relatively clear digital asset tax and compliance frameworks
- On-chain payments become the default option for cross-border projects
- Traditional payments are still used for local scenarios with clear regulatory requirements

# Chapter 9: Startup Manual — From 0 to the First TSC

## 9.1 Minimum Start Unit (MSU) Definition

The Minimum Start Unit (MSU) is the minimal configuration required to launch an effectively operating single-kernel TSC.

**MSU includes**:

1. **1 human orchestrator** (yourself, or a partner with a clear intent)
2. **1 general large model access** (Claude API or ChatGPT API)
3. **1 task management tool** (can be Notion, Feishu/Lark, or just a WeChat group)
4. **1 knowledge base tool** (Notion, Obsidian, or a local folder + Git)
5. **1 payment collection capability** (Alipay/WeChat Pay collection, or overseas Stripe)
6. **1 clear first intent** (what I want to do, for whom, and to what extent)

This is the true minimum: if you have these six things, you can start.

**MSU does not include**:
- Company registration (not required, can start with personal identity)
- A team (one person can start)
- Capital (a few hundred RMB in API fees is enough)
- Professional skills (required professional skills can be supplemented through Agents)

## 9.2 Week 1: Intent Confirmation and Role Allocation

**Day 1-2: Intent Workshop**

Spend 1-2 hours answering the following questions (using pen and paper, or writing to Claude):```
1. What am I good at? (Can be answered subjectively, the more specific the better)
2. In what field do I have more knowledge accumulation than most people?
3. In this field, who has pain points that they would pay to solve?
4. For this pain point, which parts can AI help me do?
5. Which parts must be done by me, that AI cannot replace?
6. What is the first minimum viable problem to solve?
```Do not skip this step. Many TSCs fail because the intent is unclear from the start, resulting in all subsequent resource investments being misdirected.

**Day 3-4: Toolchain Setup**

Build your foundational toolchain:```
Communication/Collaboration: WeChat group (simplest) or Telegram (if international collaboration)
Task management: Feishu Docs or Notion (free version is sufficient)
Knowledge base: Same as above, or Obsidian (local, more secure)
Large model: Claude API (recommended) or ChatGPT API
File storage: Google Drive or Baidu Netdisk (Enterprise Edition)
Payment: Alipay personal collection or Stripe (if overseas needed)
```Do not start with the most complex tools. Tools can be upgraded later, but intent cannot be remedied later.

**Day 5-7: First Agent Configuration**

Based on your intent, configure the first Agent specifically dedicated to serving this intent.

Example: If your intent is "AI consulting for SMEs," your first Agent could be:```
[System Prompt]
You are a research assistant for an AI implementation consulting advisor.
Your job is:
1. When I describe a client's business scenario, help me analyze in which links AI can provide value
2. For each possible AI application scenario, evaluate implementation difficulty (Low/Medium/High) and potential value
3. Based on the analysis, generate a short "AI Opportunity Report"

Output format:
- Scenario description (2-3 sentences)
- AI application opportunity list (for each opportunity: opportunity description, implementation difficulty, potential value)
- Recommended opportunities for priority implementation (1-2) and reasons
```Test this Agent until you are satisfied with the output quality.

## 9.3 Week 2: Toolchain Building and Deepening

**Goal**: Enable your toolchain to support a complete task workflow, from intent input to delivery output.

**Key Activity 1: Build the Knowledge Base Foundation**

Transfer the knowledge in your head to a place where it can be called by AI. This includes:

- Your industry insights and methodologies
- Past cases you have worked on (desensitized)
- Commonly used templates and frameworks
- Reference materials you consider important

You don't need to organize everything at once. Start with the "knowledge that most directly impacts the current intent," and accumulate a little every day.

**Key Activity 2: Design the First Workflow**

Walk through your first task workflow from beginning to end, and annotate each step:
- Is this step done by a human or by AI?
- If done by AI, what tool is used?
- What is the input, and what is the output?
- What is the quality standard for this step?

Use a flowchart or simple text description; no complex tools are needed.

**Key Activity 3: Find the First Real Test Subject**

Do not test your TSC in a vacuum. Find a real person willing to give you feedback (a friend, former colleague, or member of an online community), and test your workflow with a real need.

Offer your first service for free or at a low price in exchange for detailed feedback.

## 9.4 Week 3: First Real Delivery

**Principle: Done, not perfect**

For the first real delivery, the most important thing is not perfect quality, but:
1. Having a real client with a real need
2. You completed the delivery using your TSC toolchain
3. The client provides feedback on the result (whether positive or negative)
4. You can learn specific directions for improvement from this delivery

This delivery will expose the weakest link in your toolchain. This is valuable information.

**What to do immediately after delivery**:```
1. Record time consumption: how much time each step took
2. Record quality issues: where the output quality is unsatisfactory, and why
3. Record unexpected situations: what happened outside the plan
4. Record client feedback: what they think is good, bad, and what they want to change
5. Write a "This Retrospective" document (even if only 500 words)
```This retrospective document is the initial accumulation of your knowledge base.

## 9.5 Week 4: Retrospective and Structural Solidification

**Analyze Data**:

Based on the records from the third week, analyze:
- In the entire workflow, which stage consumes the most human time? (This is the area most worth automating with AI)
- Which stage has the most inconsistent quality? (This is the area most in need of optimization)
- Which part are clients most satisfied with? (This is your core competitive advantage and should be reinforced)

**Structural Solidification**:

Codify the "effective path" you discovered in the third week into a repeatable SOP (Standard Operating Procedure).

The format of the SOP is very simple:```
Task name: [Specific task]
Trigger condition: [When to execute this task]
Input: [What input is needed to start]
Execution steps: [Specific steps 1, 2, 3...]
Tools/Agents used per step: [Tool name + Prompt]
Output: [What to deliver]
Quality check: [How to determine if the output meets requirements]
```Benefits of solidifying SOPs:
- Execution time for the same type of task next time is reduced by more than 50%
- Makes it easier to hand off tasks to collaborators (human or AI)
- Is one of the most valuable contents in your knowledge base

## 9.6 Common Failure Modes and Countermeasures

**Failure Mode 1: Unclear Intent, Tools First**

Symptoms: Researching various AI tools right from the start, spending a lot of time on tool configuration, but not knowing what to use the tools for.

Root cause: Anxiety-driven action rather than intent-driven action.

Countermeasure: Before touching any tools, spend 1 hour answering "Whose problem am I solving and what is it?" Only consider tools after getting a clear answer.

---

**Failure Mode 2: Perfectionism Leading to Never Starting**

Symptoms: The toolchain is missing one feature from being fully configured; the knowledge base hasn't been completely organized; the first client hasn't been secured; waiting for everything to be ready before starting.

Root cause: Fear of failure, disguised as "insufficient preparation."

Countermeasure: Set a "good enough" launch standard instead of a perfect one. "I have an intent, a tool, a potential client"—that's enough, start.

---

**Failure Mode 3: Working Alone, Lacking External Feedback**

Symptoms: Working heads-down alone, not showing it to anyone, fearing criticism or plagiarism.

Root cause: Over-application of the Dark Forest mentality.

Countermeasure: Outside of real clients, find 1-3 people willing to give honest feedback (not necessarily industry experts, but people willing to say "this is bad"). Show progress regularly.

---

**Failure Mode 4: Over-reliance on AI, Ignoring Human Judgment**

Symptoms: AI generates a result, and it is sent directly to the client without manual review. One time the AI produces an erroneous output (hallucination), the client receives incorrect information, and the relationship is damaged.

Root cause: Over-trust in AI capabilities.

Countermeasure: Build a habit of "final human confirmation," especially before delivering to external clients. Make final confirmation a mandatory last step in the workflow.

---

**Failure Mode 5: No Accumulation, Starting from Scratch Every Time**

Symptoms: After each project ends, there is no systematic recording of experience; the next similar project still requires fumbling from scratch.

Root cause: Valuing execution while neglecting accumulation.

Countermeasure: Make "project post-mortem" and "knowledge base entry" mandatory steps after each project ends, executing them even before settlement. Your knowledge base is the most important long-term asset of the TSC.

# Chapter 10: Legal Gray Areas and Compliance Paths

## 10.1 The Legal Identity Issue of TSC

TSC 2.0 is an organizational form born outside the legal framework. The existing legal definition of a "company" is based on the following assumptions: having a fixed registered entity, having clear membership relationships (employer-employee or partners), having a fixed business scope, and having a fixed registered location.

TSC 2.0 consciously breaks these assumptions: it can have no registered entity, membership relationships are dynamic and project-based, the business scope is industry-agnostic, and participants may be distributed globally.

This is not a bug, it is a feature—but it brings real legal gray areas.

**Main Legal Gray Areas**:

**Contract Issues**: When multiple TSCs collaborate, if a dispute arises, who sues whom? Based on what contract? Under which legal system?

**Tax Issues**: Who declares the income generated by a TSC? In which country is it declared? Under what nature is it declared (salary, labor remuneration, business income)?

**Intellectual Property Issues**: Who owns the intellectual property of the content produced through TSC collaboration?

**Data Issues**: Who owns the data collected by the TSC during the process of serving clients? How is it used?

## 10.2 Practical Constraints in Mainland China

Operating a TSC 2.0 in Mainland China requires facing the following practical constraints:

**Personal Income Tax**:
- Labor remuneration: Taxed per occurrence, tax rate 20%-40%
- Business income: Register as an individual business (sole proprietorship), subject to deemed taxation or audit-based taxation
- Recommendation: For annual income exceeding 100,000 RMB, it is recommended to register as an individual business for standardized operation

**Invoice Issues**:
- Many corporate clients require invoices, and individuals cannot issue special VAT invoices
- Solutions: Issue general invoices through an individual business, or have them issued by a tax service agency on your behalf

**Cryptocurrency**:
- Mainland China prohibits cryptocurrency trading; on-chain payments are only applicable for overseas scenarios
- Converting overseas receipts into fiat currency before transferring them into the country requires compliance with foreign exchange management regulations

**Data Compliance**:
- The *Data Security Law* and the *Personal Information Protection Law* have strict regulations on data processing
- If a TSC processes a large amount of user data, it needs to establish a compliant data management process

## 10.3 Design Concepts for Offshore Structures

For larger-scale TSCs, an offshore structure is an effective path to solve cross-border settlement and certain regulatory issues.

**Common Offshore Structures**:

**Hong Kong Company**:
- Low setup cost, offshore income is tax-exempt
- Can open multi-currency bank accounts
- Can access overseas payment tools like Stripe
- Disadvantage: Cannot directly solve Mainland tax issues

**Singapore Company**:
- Startup-friendly, low tax rate (0% tax rate on the first 100,000 SGD of profit)
- Can accept global clients
- Suitable for TSCs focused on the Southeast Asian or international markets

**Cayman Islands/British Virgin Islands**:
- Suitable for TSCs requiring financing, token issuance, or complex equity structures
- Higher setup and maintenance costs, suitable for larger-scale TSCs

**Recommended Path** (for most medium-sized TSCs):
1. Startup phase: Operate under personal identity, register a sole proprietorship
2. After annual income stably exceeds 300,000 RMB: Consider registering a Hong Kong company
3. After having international clients or financing needs: Choose an offshore structure based on specific circumstances

## 10.4 Redefinition of Labor Relations

There are no "employees" in the traditional sense in TSC 2.0. Participants typically engage in one of the following ways:

**Independent Contractors**: Sign service contracts (which can be minimalist, or even oral with screenshot confirmation), settled per project. For tax purposes, the payee needs to self-declare individual income tax.

**Partners**: Core members with long-term participation and stable contributions, possibly with some form of profit-sharing agreement. In mainland China, this can be achieved through a partnership enterprise form.

**Consultants/Experts**: Provide specific professional opinions, charged per session or per time.

**Recommendation**: Do not deliberately evade labor relations, but clearly choose which relationship to adopt, and accordingly establish formal written confirmation (even if it is just a screenshot of chat records).

## 10.5 Data and Privacy Compliance Framework

TSC 2.0 will accumulate a large amount of data during operation: customer data, project data, and user behavior data. The processing of this data must comply with relevant regulations.

**Basic Compliance Requirements**:

1. **Notice and Consent**: Clearly inform users (customers and participants) what data you collect, how it is used, and how it is stored
2. **Data Minimization**: Only collect data necessary to complete the service, do not over-collect
3. **Secure Storage**: Sensitive data must be encrypted and stored, with access rights strictly controlled
4. **Right to Deletion**: Users have the right to request the deletion of their data, and the TSC must be able to respond to such requests
5. **No Sharing**: Do not share user data with third parties without user consent

**Special Attention for AI Training Data**:

When a TSC uses customer data to train or fine-tune AI models, it must confirm:
- Whether the customer data agreement permits such use
- Whether an additional data usage agreement is required

## 10.6 Strategies While Awaiting Regulatory Maturity

Regulation always lags behind technology. TSC 2.0 currently faces a regulatory vacuum period, which is both an opportunity and a risk.

**The reasonable strategy is**:

**Do the right thing, do not do illegal things**: Obey existing laws, maintain restraint and boundaries in legal vacuum zones, and do not do things that "regulation has not yet explicitly prohibited, but clearly carry risks".

**Establish Records**: Retain all important communication records, contract documents, and financial records. When the regulatory framework arrives, clear records allow you to quickly prove compliance.

**Follow Policy Dynamics**: National-level AI regulation, digital economy policies, and digital currency policies all directly impact the operating environment of TSC 2.0. Establish a mechanism to monitor these policy dynamics.

**Build Relationships with Legal Professionals**: Find a lawyer who understands technology, data, and cross-border business, and consult them before important decisions. You do not need to hire them every time, but building a relationship allows you to quickly obtain professional advice when needed.

# Chapter 11: Scaling Path—Constructing the TSC Ecosystem

## 11.1 From a Single TSC to a TSC Ecosystem

A single-kernel TSC is a force amplification tool for an individual. But when multiple TSCs begin to form a network, creating an ecosystem of mutual collaboration, mutual recommendation, and mutual enhancement, a true organizational paradigm revolution emerges.

The formation of a TSC ecosystem is not planned, but naturally emerges—provided there are enough single-kernel TSCs operating, with sufficient complementarity, sufficient foundation of trust, and sufficiently low collaboration friction among them.

**Hierarchical Structure of the TSC Ecosystem**:```
[First layer: Single-core TSC]
Quantity: Hundreds to thousands
Characteristics: Highly specialized, single-domain depth

[Second layer: Temporary consortium]
Temporarily combined by multiple single-core TSCs on a project basis
Features: born for the project, disbanded after the project ends

[Third layer: stable cluster]
A TSC group with a long-term cooperation history, forming tacit understanding and trust
Features: more stable than a consortium, but still maintains the independence of each TSC

[Fourth layer: ecosystem platform]
Provides infrastructure for collaboration between TSCs: reputation system, capability market, settlement system
Features: this is the connection layer of the entire ecosystem
```## 11.2 TSC Market: Tradable Intentions and Capabilities

A mature TSC ecosystem requires a **capability market**—allowing people with intentions to find capable TSCs, and capable TSCs to find intentions that suit them.

**Design Elements of the Capability Market**:

**Capability Description**: Each TSC should have a standardized "capability card" describing:
- What I am good at doing (capability tags)
- What I have done (case Portfolio)
- How I charge (price range or pricing logic)
- My response speed (how long before I can start after receiving a task)
- My quality assurance (what happens if not satisfied)

**Intent Publication**: The party with an intention (project requirement) can publish the intention on the market, waiting for a suitable TSC to respond.

**Matching Mechanism**: The core function of the capability market is matching—letting the right intention meet the right capability. This matching can be done manually (by community operators) or assisted by AI (recommending the most suitable TSC based on the intent description).

**Early Implementation**: No dedicated platform is needed; a WeChat group or Discord server can serve as an early capability market. The key is having sufficiently active participants and sufficiently frequent demand flow.

## 11.3 Design of the Reputation System

In the TSC ecosystem, reputation is the most important asset. The role of reputation is: to allow strangers to quickly build enough trust to start collaborating.

**Sources of Reputation**:
- Past project records (what was completed, quality, whether on time)
- Evaluations from collaborating TSCs (how TSCs that have worked together evaluate each other)
- Client evaluations (how clients who have been served evaluate)
- Knowledge contributions (whether the content shared within the ecosystem is valuable)

**Design Principles of the Reputation System**:

**Unforgeable**: Reputation evaluations must be based on real collaboration or service history and cannot be fabricated out of thin air. On-chain records (if the collaboration is settled on-chain) naturally provide tamper-proof historical records.

**Decayable**: Reputation cannot be permanent. If a TSC has no new delivery records for three years, its historical reputation should be discounted because its current capabilities may have changed.

**Multi-dimensional**: Do not use a single score to represent reputation. Reputations in different dimensions (quality, speed, communication, innovation) have different importance for different projects.

**Contestable**: When an evaluation is unjust, the evaluated TSC should have the opportunity to appeal and present counter-evidence.

## 11.4 Knowledge Accumulation and Reuse Mechanism

The most important long-term asset of the TSC ecosystem is collectively accumulated knowledge.

**Knowledge Accumulation at the Individual Level**:
Each single-kernel TSC should have its own private knowledge base, storing:
- Its own methodologies and workflow SOPs
- Cases and learnings from past projects
- Industry-specific knowledge
- Capability graphs of collaborated TSCs

**Knowledge Sharing at the Ecosystem Level**:
Some knowledge within the ecosystem can be shared through the following ways:
- Publicly shared methodologies and frameworks (similar to open-source projects)
- Evaluations and comparisons of technologies and tools (helping other TSCs choose tools)
- Anonymous sharing of failure cases (preventing others from repeating the same mistakes)

**Knowledge Monetization**:
High-quality knowledge can become a source of income for TSCs:
- Paid skill packs (similar to OpenClaw's Skill)
- Knowledge subscriptions (paying for continuous insights in specific domains)
- Consulting services (transforming knowledge advantages into high-value advisory roles)

## 11.5 Preventing TSC from Alienating into a New Bureaucracy

This is a serious warning: The original intention of TSC 2.0 was to break the bureaucracy, but as the scale expands, the TSC ecosystem itself may generate new bureaucratization trends.

**Warning Signs**:
- TSC collaboration begins to require "proposals" and "approvals"
- The reputation system begins to be manipulated by a few people
- The capability market begins to favor specific TSCs (cliquishness)
- "Norms" begin to become more important than "results"
- The threshold for joining the TSC ecosystem is increasing rather than decreasing

**Anti-alienation Mechanisms**:
- Regularly re-evaluate all rules: Every rule must have a reason to exist; rules cannot self-replicate
- Maintain a low entry threshold: The friction for new TSCs joining the ecosystem must always remain at an extremely low level
- Decentralize power: No single TSC or individual has controlling power over the entire ecosystem
- Open exit paths: Any participant can take their data and reputation records and leave at any time
- Embrace splitting: When a group of TSCs feels the existing ecosystem is not suitable for them, splitting off to establish a new ecosystem is healthy, not a threat

# Chapter 12: Anti-Traditional Enterprise Manifesto

## 12.1 The Seven Arrogances of Traditional Enterprises

This is not venting emotions; this is competitive analysis. Only by understanding the opponent's arrogance can we find the gaps to attack.

**Arrogance One: Scale Arrogance**

"We have 1,000 people, what do you have?"

This arrogance presupposes that "scale equals capability." But in the AI era, scale is starting to become a burden: 1,000 people require 1,000 salaries, 1,000 KPI evaluations, and countless meetings to coordinate. Yet a 10-person TSC, using AI, can accomplish the same or even better output.

Scale has already turned from a moat into a sunk cost.

**Arrogance Two: Seniority Arrogance**

"We've been in this industry for 30 years, what do you know?"

This arrogance presupposes that "time equals insight." But 30 years of experience, without continuous updating, can be a liability in a rapidly changing technological environment—because it makes people overly trust past experience and naturally resist new paradigms.

TSC 2.0 uses large models to compress the learning curve: an orchestrator with clear intent, paired with trained professional Agents, can acquire sufficiently deep industry knowledge within a few weeks and begin generating value.

**Arrogance Three: Process Arrogance**

"We have mature processes; any change requires evaluation and approval."

This arrogance presupposes that "process equals quality." Processes indeed reduce individual failures, but they also strip the entire organization of its ability to respond quickly to changes. In an era where AI capabilities leap forward every 6 months, an organization that needs 6 months to complete a single process improvement will forever be in a lagging state.

TSC 2.0 has no rigidified processes; each project designs its workflow based on intent, and learnings from the previous project directly enter the design of the next. The process is alive, rather than carved in stone.

**Arrogance Four: Resource Arrogance**

"We have the best talent, the most data, and the strongest financial power."

This arrogance presupposes that "resource advantages are enduring." But the best talent is starting to become independent TSCs; the value of data depends on whether it can be effectively analyzed, and AI has democratized analytical capabilities; financial advantage, in the context of AI reducing most execution costs, is no longer a decisive factor in small projects.

**Arrogance Five: Relationship Arrogance**

"Our client relationships have been built over decades; newcomers cannot get in."

This arrogance is the one closest to reality—relationships are indeed a moat. But the relationship moat will be breached under the following circumstances:

- New providers can deliver substantially better results, so good that the relationship cost is no longer worth it
- The relationship holder (usually a specific person on the purchasing side) resigns or retires
- Decision-makers on the purchasing side change generations, and the new generation is more willing to try new suppliers

TSC 2.0's offensive strategy is not to directly assault old relationships, but to **build new relationships**—within the client circles of traditional enterprises, finding those who are dissatisfied with existing suppliers, or who originally had no suppliers, or who are willing to try new approaches.

**Arrogance Six: Compliance Arrogance**

"We have the qualifications, we comply with regulations, you do not yet."

Compliance requirements are real barriers; this arrogance has a realistic basis. But TSC 2.0 can bypass this through the "TSC commands traditional enterprises" model—the compliant traditional enterprise provides the license, and the TSC provides the capability. Each gets what it needs.

**Arrogance Seven: Inertia Arrogance**

"We have always done it this way, and it has always worked."

This is the most dangerous arrogance, and also the most common. It is not an active defense, but a passive inaction. Inertia arrogance looks like wisdom in a stable environment, but in a rapidly changing environment, it is a fatal blind spot.

TSC 2.0's ultimate weapon is not better technology, but **faster iteration speed**. Every project is a learning opportunity, and every learning directly enters the next workflow design. The learning speed of traditional enterprises is limited by their own scale and processes. The learning speed of a TSC is only limited by the human orchestrator's curiosity and desire to learn.

## 12.2 TSC's Offensive Strategy Map

TSC is not trying to eliminate traditional enterprises. What TSC aims to do is: **make itself an entity that traditional enterprises must cooperate with**.

**Offensive Direction One: Enter from the Margins**

Do not directly attack the core business of traditional enterprises. Start from the businesses they ignore, undervalue, or consider marginal, and prove that TSC can do it better, faster, and cheaper. Once trust is established, then gradually penetrate towards the core business.

**Offensive Direction Two: Data First**

When cooperating with traditional enterprises, strive for data access rights. Data is the hardest asset to replicate. Once a TSC possesses deep data of a certain industry and trains professional models based on this data, it holds irreplaceable insights into that industry.

**Offensive Direction Three: Winning by Speed**

Traditional enterprises need 1-2 weeks to provide a quote to clients, whereas a TSC can deliver a preliminary proposal within 24 hours. This difference in speed, in the eyes of the client, is a massive difference in service quality. Speed is a TSC's natural competitive advantage, and it must be continuously amplified.

**Offensive Direction Four: Price Disruption**

The labor cost of a TSC is much lower than that of traditional enterprises (AI does a large amount of the execution work), which allows the TSC to offer prices 30-70% cheaper than traditional enterprises while maintaining higher profit margins. For the client, it is cheaper; for the TSC, it is more profitable—this is a true competitive advantage.

**Offensive Direction Five: Knowledge Assetization**

Turn the knowledge accumulated by TSC into sellable products: methodology courses, data reports, industry white papers, toolkits... These knowledge products can reach long-tail customers that traditional enterprises cannot serve, while simultaneously strengthening TSC's authority within the industry.

## 12.3 Which Industries Will Be Rewritten First

Not all industries will be rewritten at the same pace. The following industries are where TSC 2.0 has the best opportunity for rapid entry:

**First Batch (2026-2027)**:

**Content Production and Marketing**: The capabilities of AI writing, imaging, and video have already reached a commercial level. The labor cost advantage of traditional content companies has disappeared. TSC can achieve equal or even better output at 1/5 of the cost.

**Market Research and Data Analysis**: The traditional notion of "it takes 3 months to produce an industry report" loses its meaning in the face of AI. TSC can produce reports of equivalent quality within 3 days.

**Software Development (Basic Level)**: Claude Code and similar tools allow basic programming tasks to be completed by AI. TSC's competitiveness in the software development field comes from the orchestrator's architectural judgment and quality control, rather than the speed of writing code.

**Education and Training**: AI can generate personalized course content, and AI can act as a 24-hour Q&A assistant. The labor costs of traditional training companies are a pure burden in this scenario.

**Second Batch (2027-2029)**:

**Legal Services (Basic Level)**: Contract review, routine document drafting, regulatory inquiries—these legal tasks that can be assisted by AI will be rewritten by the combination of TSC and legal AI tools.

**Finance and Accounting (Standardized Parts)**: Bookkeeping, tax filing, simple financial analysis—AI's precision and speed exceed manual labor, allowing TSC to serve more clients at a lower cost.

**Medical Auxiliary Services**: Pre-consultation information collection, preliminary analysis of common diseases, health management advice—not core medical care, but auxiliary services, where TSC can establish a massive efficiency advantage.

**Logistics Optimization**: Route planning, warehouse management, demand forecasting—AI's capabilities in these areas already far exceed manual labor. TSC can act as the "algorithmic brain" for logistics companies, providing significant efficiency improvements.

**Third Batch (2029-2030)**:

**Manufacturing Optimization**: As the cost of humanoid robots decreases, TSC can simultaneously manage both digital and physical production processes.

**Education (Deep Personalization)**: AI Agents can provide truly personalized teaching based on each student's learning progress and style. This will rewrite the economic model of the entire education industry.

**Government Public Services**: Policy consultation, AI-driven public services—TSC can act as the government's intelligent transformation partner.

## 12.4 Who We Are, Where We Are Going

We are the people who believe the following:

We believe that **scale does not equal value**. One person with the right tools can do what 10 people do—this is not a miracle, it is leverage.

We believe that **execution is no longer scarce; judgment is what's scarce**. Knowing what you want, knowing what "good" means—this is the most valuable capability in the AI era.

We believe that **organizations can be light**. You don't need walls covered in org charts, thick rules and regulations, or annual performance reviews to enable a group of people to create value effectively.

We believe that **community is not a marketing tool, but a production tool**. True collaboration happens where there is trust, and trust happens where there are genuine connections between people.

We believe that **technology should serve intent, not rule intent**. We use AI, but the direction of AI is determined by us.

The place we are going is a world like this:

A young person with a clear idea can assemble a Thin-Shell Company within a few hours and start serving real clients. No financing needed, no hiring needed, no office needed, no dealing with any bureaucratic systems.

A small team can take on a project that traditionally required a large company to complete, because their AI execution capability compensates for the gap in headcount.

A collaborative network distributed across five countries can launch a project on the same day, produce output that very day, settle accounts in real-time upon completion, and then recombine in a completely different way for the next project.

A traditional enterprise can choose to collaborate with TSC, gaining the agility it could never cultivate internally, while retaining its unique assets and access advantages.

This world is not a utopia. It is the natural extension of current trends. It will also have its own problems: power concentration, knowledge monopoly, reputation manipulation... These problems need to be taken seriously; we cannot pretend they do not exist.

But it offers more people a more real opportunity than the world we currently live in: to participate in the construction of the world using capability rather than seniority, value rather than connections, and intent rather than identity.

This is what Thin-Shell Company 2.0 wants to create in the world.

# Appendix A: Core Glossary

**Thin-Shell Company 2.0 (TSC 2.0)**  
A novel organizational form with intent as the starting point, AI swarms as the execution layer, human judgment as the guide, community as the organizational interface, and dual-track settlement as the value exchange mechanism.

**Intent Layer**  
A new human-computer interaction paradigm: users express needs in natural language, AI understands the intent and routes it to the correct execution layer, and users do not need to understand the underlying tools and processes.

**Orchestrator**  
The human role in TSC responsible for transforming intent into task sequences, coordinating Agent execution, and judging execution quality. The most scarce role in TSC 2.0.

**Nested TSC**  
Multiple single-kernel TSCs temporarily combine on a project basis, with one acting as the lead orchestrator and the others acting as execution units. After the project ends, each TSC resumes independence.

**Brain-Limb Model**  
TSC acts as the "brain" with cognitive and algorithmic advantages, while traditional enterprises act as the "limbs" with physical assets and access qualifications, forming a mutually complementary cooperative structure (corresponding to Structure C).

**Dual-Track Payment**  
The financial architecture during the TSC 2.0 transition period that simultaneously supports traditional payments (WeChat, Alipay, banks) and on-chain payments (stablecoins, smart contracts).

**Psychological Contract**  
Mutual expectations within TSC that are not explicitly written down but are recognized by all participants. Includes: contributions being respected, fair and transparent settlement, and exit without penalty, etc.

**Silent Production**  
The working mode where AI Agents continuously work unsupervised, producing output while humans rest.

**Skill Evaporation**  
The phenomenon where a certain type of skill loses market value extremely quickly due to the improvement of AI capabilities. Distinguished from slow depreciation.

**Knowledge Moat**  
The competitive barrier formed by TSC through a long-term accumulated private knowledge base (industry insights, customer history, proprietary methodologies). Harder to replicate than tools and skills.

**Minimum Start Unit (MSU)**  
The minimum configuration required to start an effectively operating single-kernel TSC: 1 orchestrator + large model access + task management tool + knowledge base + payment collection capability + first intent.

**Capability Market**  
A platform or mechanism within the TSC ecosystem for those with intent to find capable TSCs, and for capable TSCs to find suitable intents.

**Reputation System**  
A mechanism that records TSC's historical delivery quality, collaboration experience, and knowledge contributions, used to build trust among strangers within the TSC ecosystem.
